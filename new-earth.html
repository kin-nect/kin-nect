Ebers Papyrus — c.1550 BCE

110 pages, 20 metres long — the most comprehensive medical document from the ancient world. Written in hieratic script, it was copied from even older texts dating to around 3400 BCE. It covers internal medicine, dermatology, gynaecology, dentistry, and psychiatry — treating the body as a complete system.

Edwin Smith Papyrus — c.1600 BCE
World's oldest surgical textbook · 48 trauma cases · first use of the word "brain"
▼
The most rational medical text from the ancient world — no magic, no incantations, just clinical observation. 48 cases of trauma are documented in systematic order from head to foot. Each case follows the same structure: examination, diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment. Originally from the Old Kingdom (c.3000 BCE), making it potentially 5,000 years old. Attributed to Imhotep.
First text in history to use the word "brain" and describe its relationship to bodily function
Documents the observation that brain injuries on one side of the head cause symptoms on the opposite side of the body — contralateral neurological function, rediscovered by European medicine in the 19th century
48 cases: head fractures, spinal injuries, dislocations, tumours, open wounds — all with rational, evidence-based treatment
Surgical sutures, wound closure with adhesive tape, and splinting of fractures all documented
Distinguishes between treatable, uncertain, and untreatable conditions — the first medical triage system
Held at: New York Academy of Medicine · Research: The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus Vol.1 — James Henry Breasted, Oriental Institute, University of Chicago (available via Oriental Institute publications)
🌸
Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus — c.1825 BCE
Oldest medical text in Egyptian history · women's health · fertility · contraception
▼
The oldest known medical text from ancient Egypt — predating the Ebers by 275 years. 34 sections address women's health exclusively: gynaecology, fertility, pregnancy complications, contraception, and infant care. It acknowledges that many conditions were systemic — that a problem presenting in one part of the body may originate in another. This systemic understanding of the body did not reappear in Western medicine until the 20th century.
Contraceptive methods documented: crocodile dung pessary, acacia and honey (both have documented medical basis)
Pregnancy testing: placing an onion inside the vagina overnight — if the woman's breath smelled of onion by morning, she was not pregnant (referenced in later Greek medical texts)
Treatment for painful menstruation, uterine prolapse, and gynaecological infections
Infant feeding instructions and weaning protocols
Held at: Petrie Museum of Egyptian Archaeology, University College London · Research: The Gynaecology of the Kahun Papyrus — S. Blackwell

Governance — how Ma'at was implemented

Ma'at as constitution
Not a written code — a lived standard · the feather of Ma'at · pharaoh's daily obligation
▼
Ma'at was not a written law code. It was a lived standard — the cosmic principle of truth, balance, and justice that every person in Egyptian society was expected to embody in their daily actions. Every official wore or displayed Ma'at's feather as their oath of office. The pharaoh was required to "offer Ma'at daily" — their legitimacy depended on demonstrating justice and balance, not just holding power. A pharaoh who violated Ma'at lost divine favour and risked political collapse. This is governance rooted in cosmic law rather than human authority — the ruler serves the principle, not the other way around.
Research: Ma'at: The Moral Ideal in Ancient Egypt — Maulana Karenga (University of Sankore Press, 2004) · The Role of Ma'at in Ancient Egyptian Law — Brewminate.com
👁️
The Vizier — law required knowledge, not rank
"Do not act wilfully in cases where the law is unknown to you" · checks on pharaoh's power
▼
The Vizier was the pharaoh's chief administrator and supreme judge — the most powerful government official below the pharaoh himself. Viziers were explicitly instructed in their oath of appointment: "Do not act willfully in cases where the law is unknown to you; acquaint yourself with it." Authority came from knowledge, not rank. The Vizier also served as a check on the pharaoh's power in practice — administering justice independently and reporting on the state of the kingdom without fear of the pharaoh's displeasure. A governance model where the administrator's authority derives from knowledge of the law, not personal loyalty to the ruler.
Research: The Vizier of Upper and Lower Egypt — T.G.H. James, Journal of Egyptian Archaeology · Ancient Egyptian Administration — Juan Carlos Moreno García (ed.), Brill, 2013

The courts — priests of Ma'at · restorative justice 3,000 years before the term
Precedent · moral instruction · restoration of balance · never punishment alone
▼
Judges in Kemet were called "priests of Ma'at." They did not apply a written legal code — they applied precedent and moral principle, always with the goal of restoring balance rather than punishing wrongdoing. The question was never simply "who broke the rule?" but "what has been disrupted, and what would restore harmony?" Witnesses swore oaths on Ma'at. False testimony was considered a cosmic violation, not merely a procedural one. This is restorative justice — 3,000 years before the modern term was coined. The New Earth's community conflict resolution circle is rooted in this exact tradition.
Research: Law and Society in Ancient Egypt — Battiscombe Gunn · Crime and Punishment in Ancient Egypt — Alan Moores

In daily life — Ma'at as the operating system
Accurate weights · truth in disputes · care for community · cosmic consequences
▼
Ma'at was not reserved for courts and temples. It governed daily commercial and social life. An Egyptian merchant using false weights was not just breaking a regulation — they were violating cosmic law. Cheating a customer disrupted Ma'at. Treating a worker unjustly disrupted Ma'at. Every transaction, every relationship, every interaction was understood as either upholding or undermining the cosmic order. This meant that ethics was not separate from commerce, not separate from governance, not separate from spirituality. They were the same thing, described in different domains.

Mali Empire & Timbuktu

Books were the most valuable commodity in Timbuktu. Merchants who arrived to trade gold and salt left having spent more on manuscripts than on any other goods. Knowledge was the founding principle of this civilisation — not wealth, not military power. Knowledge first. Everything else followed.
The founding principle of Timbuktu

Mansa Musa's Mali Empire was simultaneously the wealthiest and the most educated civilisation on Earth. Timbuktu was not a city of gold — it was a city of knowledge. A single pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324 distributed so much gold it caused inflation across North Africa and the Middle East for twelve years. He used that wealth to build universities. In 1324, Oxford had existed for 200 years. Sankore had been running for over 300.

What they studied — select a subject
LAW — MANDATORY STUDY
Islamic law, jurisprudence & the science of justice
Law was the core of the Sankore curriculum — not as a set of rules to memorise, but as a living discipline of ethical reasoning. Students studied the Maliki school of jurisprudence, learning how to apply divine principles to real disputes: inheritance, contracts, property, marriage, governance, and criminal justice. Judges in Timbuktu were also scholars — their authority came from their depth of knowledge, not their rank. The concept that law and scholarship are inseparable is one of the most important things Timbuktu has to teach. A community that cannot reason through its own disputes from its own principles is not yet sovereign.

The circulating dollar — how it worked
19–36 times before leaving · how to recreate this
▼
When a dollar enters a community and is spent at a local business, the business owner then spends it at another local supplier, who pays a local employee, who buys groceries at a local shop — and so on. Each time the dollar moves within the community before leaving, it generates economic activity, tax revenue, employment, and wealth for community members. In Greenwood, this happened 19–36 times. In mainstream Black communities today, the dollar leaves after approximately 6 hours — spent once at a non-Black business and gone. The difference is not income. It is intention and infrastructure.
How to recreate this today
Directory first — every community member lists their skills and business. Before spending externally, check the directory. Kin-nect is this directory.
Conscious pledge — commit publicly to spending inside the community first. The Tulsa community did this through social pressure and cultural expectation, not rules.
Community currency or time-banking — an internal currency that can only be spent within the network accelerates circulation and makes it measurable
Track it — measure the internal circulation rate. Set community targets. Celebrate improvements. Make it visible.
Build missing services — every time money leaves for a service the community doesn't have, that's the next business to start inside it

🏦
Credit & banking within — no external dependency
How Greenwood financed itself · how to do it now
▼
Greenwood's banks extended credit to Black business owners who were denied loans by white institutions. They accepted deposits from the community and reinvested them as loans back into the community. This closed loop meant that community savings became community investment — wealth generated inside stayed inside. Interest paid on loans stayed inside the community rather than flowing to external shareholders.
How to build this now — UK context
Credit union — the most direct equivalent. Members pool savings; the pool lends to members at low interest. Credit unions are legally recognised in the UK, regulated by the PRA and FCA, and protected by FSCS up to £85,000. The Caribbean community specifically had strong credit union traditions (known as "pardner" or "sou-sou" savings pools).
Community Development Finance Institution (CDFI) — lends to businesses and individuals excluded from mainstream banking. Several Black-led CDFIs exist in the UK, including Transmit Startups and others. Look up CDFA (Community Development Finance Association).
Pardner / sou-sou — the traditional Caribbean rotating savings circle. 10 people each contribute £100/month. Each month one person receives the full £1,000. Zero interest. Zero bank. Completely internal. Still operating in UK Caribbean communities today.
Community bond issuance — communities can legally issue bonds to their own members to raise capital for land, buildings, or businesses. Used by community land trusts across the UK.
UK resources: Credit Union Development Association (cuda.co.uk) · Community Development Finance Association (cdfa.org.uk) · Financial Conduct Authority credit union register
Why it was destroyed — June 1, 1921
A white mob, aided by local police, the Oklahoma National Guard, and private aircraft, burned 35 blocks to the ground. 300+ residents killed. 10,000 made homeless. $150 million in today's money destroyed overnight. Then zoning laws were changed to prevent rebuilding. The message was explicit: a fully self-sufficient Black community is an existential threat to any system that profits from Black dependency. You do not destroy what does not threaten you.
What we take from Black Wall Street
The circulating dollar is the single most replicable lesson. Every time you spend inside the community, it multiplies. Every time it leaves, it's gone. The complete institution model: every service your community needs must exist inside it. Conscious economic loyalty: Greenwood didn't happen by accident — people chose each other deliberately. And the protection lesson: the New Earth must be distributed, legally protected, and networked so no single act can destroy the whole.
1825 – 1857 · Manhattan's first significant Black property-owning community

Seneca Village — Central Park

Andrew Williams was 25 years old, a shoeshiner. In September 1825 he paid $125 for three lots of land in upper Manhattan. Within weeks, a church bought adjacent land. Within years, a village existed. The founding principle: begin before you are ready, with less than you think you need.
The founding principle of Seneca Village — one person, one act, one beginning

A 25-year-old shoeshiner named Andrew Williams bought the first three lots for $125 in 1825 — two years before slavery was even abolished in New York State. Within weeks, a church had purchased land. Within years, a village existed. By 1855: 50 homes, 3 churches, 2 schools, gardens, livestock, and a natural spring. This is how fast a community can be built when people move with intention.

What was actually built
50 homes — mostly two-storey, stone foundation walls confirmed by archaeologists
3 churches — community spiritual and social centres
2 schools — including one for Black children excluded from city schools
2 cemeteries — community burial on their own land
Streets, barns, gardens, and livestock pens
How they lived
Gardens and livestock — partial food sovereignty from day one
Hudson River fishing — free protein from the surrounding land
Tanner's Spring — natural water source on the land itself
Children attended school — education a community priority
Multiracial — Black, Irish, and German families living side by side
Why it was destroyed — 1857
The city wanted the land for Central Park. The community was labelled a "shantytown" and residents were called "squatters and vagabonds" in the press — the same language used to justify displacement throughout history. Eminent domain was used. Residents fought for two years. Police removed those who resisted by force. When they left, losing land meant losing the right to vote. The destruction erased a community and its political power simultaneously. No photographs of Seneca Village exist.
What we take from Seneca Village
Land + water source + food growing + school + church is the complete minimum viable community. It can be built faster than you think — from zero to 50 homes and three churches in 30 years, starting with one shoeshiner's $125. Community Land Trusts exist precisely to prevent eminent domain — the land held collectively, so no individual can be bought out and no single act can dispossess everyone. This time, the structure protects the vision.

The founding principle of the Maroon communities — sovereignty is not negotiated, it is established

The spiritual & inner foundation

Before a single brick is laid or a seed planted, the inner ground must be prepared. Every great civilisation — Kemet, Mali, the Maroons — built its physical world on a spiritual operating system. The New Earth is no different.

What is a spiritual foundation — really?

Not religion. Not performance. The operating system beneath everything. The thing that answers: Why do we build this? What holds us together when it gets hard? What do we owe each other? What do we owe those who come after?

In Kemet, Ma'at was not a belief system people chose to adopt. It was the air. It governed the stars, the floods, the courts, the medicine, the buildings, the harvest, and the way people greeted each other. There was no separation between spiritual life and daily life. The New Earth cannot have that separation either.

The three layers
Every strong spiritual foundation operates at three levels: Personal — the individual's inner life, healing, and relationship to the Most High. Relational — how people treat each other, resolve conflict, and hold each other accountable. Cosmic — the community's relationship to land, ancestors, seasons, and the divine order. All three must be built. Inner first, then relational, then cosmic.

The individualism trap
The greatest weapon the system used against Black communities was individualism — the idea that your success is yours alone, and others' failures are theirs alone. Ubuntu is the direct antidote. But it must be consciously chosen, because the dominant culture programmes the opposite from birth. The inner work of Ubuntu is catching yourself thinking "mine" when the truth is "ours."

Ubuntu is not just a spiritual principle — it is an economic one. Cooperative land ownership is Ubuntu in practice. Sharing seeds is Ubuntu. The circulating dollar of Black Wall Street was Ubuntu. When you understand that your neighbour's prosperity is your prosperity, you stop competing and start compounding. This is why Greenwood's dollar circulated 19–36 times — people were practising Ubuntu economically without necessarily naming it.

The Hermetic tradition and Kemet
The Hermetic philosophy — attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, the Greek name for the Egyptian god Thoth — is believed by many scholars to have originated in ancient Egypt. Thoth was the Kemetic god of wisdom, writing, magic, and the moon — the scribe of the gods, the keeper of cosmic records. The Hermetic texts were written down in Greek between 100–300 CE but claim much older Egyptian origins. The phrase "as within, so without" is Hermetic. The seven laws are Hermetic. And Hermeticism, most scholars now agree, is Kemetic wisdom transmitted through a Greek name. Your ancestors wrote these laws. They belong to you.
Integral figures

The people who changed everything

Scientists, inventors, healers, queens, warriors, thinkers, and builders. Every person below changed the world. Most were never taught in school. Tap any card to go deeper.

The ancient world was shaped by African minds. These are not myths — they are documented historical figures whose contributions were later attributed to others, renamed, or simply erased.

c.2650 BCE · Kemet
Imhotep
Architect · Physician · Engineer · First named genius in recorded history
Imhotep designed the Step Pyramid of Djoser at Saqqara — the world's first large-scale stone structure. He was also chief physician to Pharaoh Djoser, an architect, astronomer, and philosopher. Deified after his death, he was worshipped as a god of medicine for 3,000 years. The Greeks later renamed him Asclepius and made him their own god of medicine. Hippocrates — credited as the father of medicine — was writing 2,000 years after Imhotep had already documented medicine systematically. Research: Imhotep the African — Molefi Kete Asante
c.2350 BCE · Kemet
Amenhotep, Son of Hapu
Architect · Philosopher · Healer · Second most revered sage of ancient Egypt
Chief architect to Pharaoh Amenhotep III, he oversaw the construction of the Colossi of Memnon and the Luxor temple complex. He was one of only two commoners to be deified after death. His philosophical writings influenced Egyptian thought for centuries. A direct ancestor of the tradition Imhotep began — genius recognised and honoured within a civilisation that understood what genius was for. Research: Ancient Egyptian Science — Marshall Clagett (3 volumes)
c.1550 BCE · Kemet
Merit-Ptah
Physician · First named woman in the history of science
Chief physician to the Pharaoh around 2700 BCE — the first woman in history known by name to have practised medicine. Her image was placed in her son's tomb with the inscription "chief physician." Predates Hippocrates by over 2,000 years. The fact that a woman was chief physician tells you something important about how Kemet actually worked: Ma'at did not discriminate by sex. Competence was the standard. Research: Women in Science — H.J. Mozans · Ancient Egyptian Medical Papyri
c.1350 BCE · Kemet
Nefertiti
Queen · Co-ruler · Political architect of the Amarna period
Wife of Pharaoh Akhenaten, Nefertiti was likely the most powerful woman in ancient Egypt. Evidence suggests she may have ruled as Pharaoh herself after her husband's death under the name Neferneferuaten. She co-authored one of history's most radical political experiments: the shift to monotheism under Aten. Whether that experiment was visionary or destructive is debated — but her power and influence are not. Research: Nefertiti: Egypt's Sun Queen — Joyce Tyldesley
c.1700s BCE · Kemet & Nubia
Ahmose-Nefertari
Queen · Founder of a religious order · Patron of artisans
Queen of Egypt during the 18th Dynasty and one of the most venerated women in Egyptian history. Deified after her death, she was worshipped for over 500 years — longer than most pharaohs. She founded and led a religious order, negotiated with the pharaoh for rights for the priesthood, and was patron of the craftsmen who built the royal tombs. Depicted with black skin — a mark of divinity and fertility in Egyptian art. Research: The Oxford History of Ancient Egypt — Ian Shaw (ed.)
c.51 BCE · Kemet
Cleopatra VII
Pharaoh · Linguist · Political strategist · Last ruler of independent Egypt
The last active ruler of the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Egypt. Spoke nine languages — the only Ptolemaic ruler to learn Egyptian. A trained mathematician and philosopher. She was not a passive figure manipulated by Roman men — she was the one manipulating Rome, using Caesar and Antony as political tools to maintain Egyptian independence for as long as possible. When she failed, she chose death over captivity and a Roman triumph. Research: Cleopatra: A Life — Stacy Schiff
c.400 BCE · Carthage (modern Tunisia)
Mago of Carthage
Agricultural scientist · Author of the world's first agricultural encyclopaedia
Mago wrote a 28-volume encyclopaedia on agriculture — so highly regarded that when Rome destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, the Roman Senate ordered Mago's work to be translated into Latin and distributed across the empire. It was considered the foundational text of European agriculture for centuries. An African scientist whose work fed Rome. Research: Roman Agriculture — K.D. White · Encyclopaedia Britannica entry on Mago
c.1312 CE · Mali Empire
Mansa Musa I
Emperor · Patron of learning · Wealthiest individual in recorded history
Emperor of the Mali Empire from 1312 to 1337. His 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca — with 60,000 people and tonnes of gold — caused inflation across North Africa and the Middle East for twelve years. He used his wealth to build universities, import scholars, and expand the libraries of Timbuktu. His empire spanned modern-day Mali, Senegal, Gambia, Guinea, Niger, Nigeria, Chad, and Mauritania. Some historians estimate his wealth at $400 billion in today's terms. Research: Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire — John Hunwick

Without Latimer's filament, electric light would have remained a laboratory curiosity. He also drafted the patent drawings for Alexander Graham Bell's telephone. He was the only Black member of Edison's elite team of inventors. His name appears on none of the famous patents. Research: Lewis Howard Latimer — Winifred Norman & Lily Patterson
1864–1943 · Missouri, USA
George Washington Carver
Agricultural scientist · Botanist · Inventor of 300+ peanut products
Developed over 300 products from peanuts and 100 from sweet potatoes — dyes, plastics, fuel, cosmetics, medicines — as part of a deliberate strategy to free Black Southern farmers from dependency on cotton. Inventor · Traffic signal · Gas mask · Safety hood

Invented the safety hood (precursor to the gas mask) and personally used it to rescue workers trapped in a tunnel under Lake Erie in 1916. Sold his traffic signal patent to General Electric for $40,000. When his identity became known after the tunnel rescue, some Southern cities cancelled their orders for his safety hood. 1918–2020 · Virginia, USA
Katherine Johnson
Mathematician · NASA · Calculated trajectories for moon landing
Her orbital mechanics calculations were critical to the success of the first US crewed spaceflights. John Glenn refused to fly unless Katherine Johnson personally verified the computer's calculations. She calculated the trajectory for Apollo 11 — the moon landing. 1891–1923 · Texas, USA
Bessie Coleman
Aviator · First Black woman to hold a pilot's licence
Denied flight training in the US because she was Black and a woman, she taught herself French, moved to France, and earned her international pilot's licence in 1921 — the first Black woman anywhere in the world to do so. She returned to the US and performed aerial stunts to raise money to open a flight school for Black Americans. She died in a plane crash at 34, before the school was built. 1875–1965 · USA & Caribbean
Elijah McCoy
Mechanical engineer · Inventor · "The Real McCoy"
Invented automatic lubrication devices for steam engines — allowing trains to run without stopping to be oiled manually, transforming rail travel efficiency. He held 57 patents. Engineers specifying his devices over inferior imitations coined the phrase "the real McCoy." Born in Canada to formerly enslaved parents who escaped via the Underground Railroad, he could only find work as a fireman despite his engineering degree. Chemist · Synthesised cortisone · Pioneer of steroid chemistry
Synthesised physostigmine from the Calabar bean, pioneering the treatment of glaucoma. Trinidadian-born communist, feminist, and Black liberation thinker. Political philosopher · Pan-Africanist · Founded the UNIA

1923–2005 · Senegal
Cheikh Anta Diop
Historian · Anthropologist · Physicist · Proved Ancient Egypt was Black African
Produced the first rigorous scientific and historical evidence that ancient Egyptian civilisation was Black African — using melanin testing of mummies, linguistic analysis, and cultural comparison. Reparations

What we are owed — and how we take it back

Everyone but us was given reparations. The question is not whether we are owed. We are. The question is: what do we do while we wait — and what do we build so we no longer have to?

The case — what was taken

Reparations is not a radical idea. It is a legal and moral principle applied to every other major historical injustice except one.

⛓️
The transatlantic slave trade — what was actually extracted
1619–1865 · estimated 12.5 million people · $14 trillion in unpaid labour
▼
The transatlantic slave trade forced approximately 12.5 million Africans into the Americas and Caribbean between 1619 and 1865. An estimated 1.8 million died in transit. Those who survived were worked without pay, without rights, without freedom, for generations. Economists Thomas Craemer (University of Connecticut) and William Darity (Duke University) have calculated the value of unpaid enslaved labour in the US alone at approximately $14 trillion in today's terms. This does not include the psychological damage, the cultural destruction, the land theft, or the century of legal discrimination that followed emancipation.
Research: A Formal Approach to Reparations — Thomas Craemer, Political Science & Politics · From Here to Equality — William Darity Jr & A. Kirsten Mullen
📋
After emancipation — 100 years of legal theft
Reconstruction · Jim Crow · redlining · urban renewal · mass incarceration
▼
The promise of Reconstruction (1865–1877) — 40 acres and a mule, land redistribution, political representation — was reversed within 12 years. What followed was 100 years of: Black Codes and convict leasing (slavery by another name), Jim Crow segregation enforced by law and violence, the destruction of thriving Black communities (Black Wall Street, Rosewood, Seneca Village), redlining (the deliberate exclusion of Black people from the government-backed mortgage system that built white middle-class wealth from the 1930s–1960s), urban renewal that destroyed Black neighbourhoods for highways, and mass incarceration that disproportionately imprisons Black Americans at rates 5x the white population. Each of these represents a distinct and calculable economic harm, layered on top of the original injustice of slavery.
Research: The Color of Law — Richard Rothstein · From Here to Equality — William Darity Jr & A. Kirsten Mullen · The New Jim Crow — Michelle Alexander
📊
The wealth gap — where it stands today
The median Black family has 1/8 the wealth of the median white family
▼
The median white family in the US holds approximately $188,000 in wealth. The median Black family holds approximately $24,000. This is not a gap caused by individual choices. It is the accumulated, compounded result of 400 years of extraction, prevention, and destruction of Black wealth. Every decade of the racial wealth gap is traceable to a specific government policy, legal structure, or act of violence that prevented Black wealth accumulation. In the UK, the picture is similar: Black British households have significantly lower wealth, homeownership rates, and pension savings than white households — despite Black Caribbean communities having been in the UK for over 70 years. The gap is not closing. Without intervention, it will take over 200 years to close at the current rate of change.
Research: UK: Race Disparity Audit — Cabinet Office · The Colour of Money — Runnymede Trust · US: Survey of Consumer Finances — Federal Reserve

What others received

Reparations have been paid before. Multiple times. To multiple groups. The precedent exists. What is missing is the political will — and the reason for its absence is instructive.

✡️
Germany to Israel and Jewish survivors — 1952
$845 million at the time · equivalent to billions today · shaped modern Israel
▼
In 1952, West Germany signed the Luxembourg Agreement, paying reparations of 3 billion deutschmarks (approximately $845 million at the time) to the State of Israel and 450 million deutschmarks to the World Jewish Congress for the benefit of Jewish survivors outside Israel. Subsequent agreements extended payments to individual survivors. These payments are widely credited as a significant contributor to Israel's early economic development. Germany has paid over $70 billion in total Holocaust reparations — and continues to pay to living survivors. The principle: the perpetrator state acknowledges the harm, quantifies it, and transfers wealth.
Research: Holocaust Reparations — Ronald Zweig · German Reparations — Nicholas Balabkins
🇺🇸
Japanese Americans — internment reparations, 1988
$20,000 per survivor · official apology · Civil Liberties Act
▼
The Civil Liberties Act of 1988, signed by President Reagan, provided $20,000 in reparations to each surviving Japanese American who had been interned during World War II — approximately 82,000 people. It also included an official government apology acknowledging that the internment was "a grave injustice." Total payments: approximately $1.6 billion. The campaign for this took 40 years of sustained political organising by Japanese American communities and their allies. The lesson: reparations require political organising sustained across generations, not just moral argument.
Research: Achieving the Impossible Dream: How Japanese Americans Obtained Redress — Mitchell Maki · Civil Liberties Act 1988 — Congressional Record
🏛️
The Marshall Plan — Europe rebuilt after World War II
$13 billion (1948) · $150 billion today · rebuilt European economies
▼
The European Recovery Program (Marshall Plan) provided $13.3 billion between 1948 and 1952 to rebuild Western European economies devastated by World War II. Adjusted for inflation, this is approximately $150 billion today. This investment in infrastructure, industry, and economic stability is widely credited as the foundation of post-war European prosperity. The same United States that invested in European reconstruction simultaneously maintained legal racial segregation at home and denied Black veterans the benefits of the GI Bill that were building white middle-class wealth. The contrast is not incidental. It is the policy.
Research: The Marshall Plan — Michael Hogan · The GI Bill and Black Veterans — Ira Katznelson, When Affirmative Action Was White
🌍
Florida's Rosewood reparations — 1994
First reparations for racial violence in American history
▼
In 1994, the Florida Legislature passed the Rosewood Claims Bill — the first legislation in American history to pay reparations for a racial massacre. It provided $150,000 to each of nine documented survivors and $500,000 to be shared among documented descendants of residents who had lost property. A further $1.5 million was provided for Black student scholarships. Total: approximately $2.1 million — a fraction of what was destroyed. But it established the principle. It happened because Arnett Doctor, a descendant of a Rosewood survivor, spent years organising, documenting, and forcing the story back into public memory. Moral argument alone did not do it. Organisation and persistence did.
Research: Like Judgment Day — Michael D'Orso · Florida Rosewood Claims Act 1994 — Florida Legislature archives

The UK specifically

Britain profited from slavery more than almost any other nation. The facts of what happened — and what was paid and to whom — are more extraordinary than most British people have been taught.

💷
Abolition 1833 — who received compensation
£20 million to slaveholders · nothing to enslaved people · paid off in 2015
▼
When Britain abolished slavery in 1833, Parliament paid £20 million in compensation — not to enslaved people, but to slaveholders, to compensate them for the "loss of their property." This was 40% of the entire government budget at the time. The debt was so large it was structured as a government loan. That loan was not fully repaid until February 2015. Until 2015, British taxpayers — including Black British taxpayers — were paying off the compensation awarded to the people who had enslaved their ancestors. This was revealed publicly by the UK government's own @HMTreasury Twitter account in a tweet that was deleted within hours after public outcry. It was not a secret. It was simply never taught.
Research: Britain's Slave Trade — S.I. Martin · Legacies of British Slave-ownership database — University College London (ucl.ac.uk/lbs) — searchable database of every slaveholder who received compensation
🏛️
Wealth built on slavery — in buildings you walk past today
UCL database · country houses · banks · insurance companies · universities
▼
The Legacies of British Slave-ownership database at University College London (ucl.ac.uk/lbs) documents every slaveholder who received compensation in 1833 and traces where that wealth went. This wealth built the institutions and infrastructure of modern Britain — while the descendants of the enslaved received nothing.

Community resilience — lessons from Jewish community structure

This section is not about comparison or competition. It is about studying what works. Tzedakah — obligatory mutual aid
Not charity · a legal obligation · 10% of income redistributed within community
▼
Tzedakah (literally "justice" or "righteousness") is not optional charity in Jewish tradition — it is a legal and moral obligation. The principle: 10% of income is to be given to support those in need within the community. This creates a systematic internal transfer of wealth that means no community member is left without support. Coupled with the principle that this giving should be done anonymously (Maimonides' highest form of tzedakah), it removes the shame and hierarchy from receiving. The community holds each other without fanfare. The Ubuntu equivalent: "When one is in crisis, the community responds — not charities, not services."
New Earth application: Community fund with regular contribution from all members. Sliding scale for services — those who have more pay more, those who have less pay less. The gift economy for basics within the network.

Observant Jewish households can only buy from kosher-certified suppliers, which are overwhelmingly Jewish-owned. This creates an internal economic circulation that keeps money within the community. The principle is the same as Black Wall Street's conscious economic loyalty — but it is structured into daily practice rather than depending on individual willpower. The New Earth equivalent: a community directory, a "buy Black first" pledge, and the social expectation that community members check the network before spending externally.
New Earth application: Kin-nect marketplace and practitioner directory. Community pledge to check the directory first. Social recognition for community economic loyalty — not guilt, but celebration.

Beth Din — internal dispute resolution
Community court · disputes resolved internally · no external legal system needed
▼
The Beth Din (literally "house of judgement") is a rabbinical court that resolves disputes within the Jewish community — commercial, civil, and personal — using Jewish law and community-appointed judges. It has legal standing in the UK under the Arbitration Act 1996, meaning its decisions are legally binding and enforceable. This means the community can resolve disputes without subjecting its members to a legal system that was not designed for them and does not always serve them. The principle: a community with its own dispute resolution system is more sovereign than one that must always turn outward for justice.
New Earth application: The community charter must include a clear conflict resolution process. The restorative justice circle as the internal dispute resolution model. For significant commercial disputes, explore arbitration agreements between community businesses.

3100 BCE – 600 CE

Ancient Africa — the first civilisations

The world's oldest civilisations were African. Before Greece, before Rome, before any European power — there was Kemet, Nubia, Carthage, Aksum. Everything that came after borrowed from what was built here.

c.9600 BCE — debated
Atlantis — the lost civilisation before recorded history
Location unknown · Atlantic Ocean? · Antarctica? · Mediterranean?
▼
Atlantis was first described by the Greek philosopher Plato in two dialogues — Timaeus and Critias — written around 360 BCE. Plato describes it as a naval power of extraordinary advancement that sank into the ocean "in a single day and night of misfortune" approximately 9,000 years before his own time — placing it around 9600 BCE. He describes it as larger than Libya and Asia combined, located beyond the Pillars of Hercules (the Strait of Gibraltar), and as the dominant civilisation of its era. Plato claimed the story came from Egyptian priests at Sais, who told the Athenian statesman Solon that the Egyptians had records going back far beyond Greek memory.
Whether Atlantis was a literal place, an allegorical construction, or a mythologised memory of real civilisations (some researchers propose it reflects folk memory of the rapid sea-level rise after the last Ice Age, which submerged vast habitable coastal areas between 10,000 and 6,000 BCE) remains genuinely unresolved. Serious scholars have proposed sites ranging from the Atlantic Ocean floor to the Canary Islands to Antarctica to Santorini (which experienced a catastrophic volcanic eruption around 1600 BCE). The Egyptian connection — that Egyptian priests held knowledge of this pre-history — places it directly within the ancestral record of the African knowledge tradition.
Why it matters for this timeline: If Plato's Egyptian source is accurate, it means African (Egyptian) civilisation preserved records of human history going back at least 9,600 BCE — thousands of years before any other written historical tradition. It also raises the possibility that the civilisations we know — Kemet, Sumer, the Indus Valley — are themselves rebuildings after an earlier, lost civilisation. The New Earth may not be the first time this has been attempted.
Source: Plato — Timaeus (360 BCE) and Critias (360 BCE) · Available free at classics.mit.edu · Ignatius Donnelly — Atlantis: The Antediluvian World (1882) · Graham Hancock — Fingerprints of the Gods (Heinemann, 1995) · Rand and Rose Flem-Ath — When the Sky Fell: In Search of Atlantis (St. Martin's Press, 1995) · Academic scepticism: John Luce — The End of Atlantis (Thames & Hudson, 1969)
c.3100 BCE
King Narmer unifies Upper and Lower Egypt — Kemet is born
Nile Valley, North Africa
▼
The first nation-state in recorded history. Narmer — also known as Menes — unified the two kingdoms of Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing a centralised state that would endure for over 3,000 years. He is depicted on the Narmer Palette, one of the earliest historical documents. This was not conquest for conquest's sake: unification created the conditions for one of the most sophisticated civilisations Earth has ever seen.
Why it matters: 3,000 years of continuous civilisation. Every institution we are trying to build — governance, medicine, education, economics, spirituality integrated as one — was functioning in Kemet. This is not inspiration. This is proof.

c.2650 BCE
Imhotep designs the Step Pyramid — the world's first large-scale stone structure
Saqqara, Kemet
▼
Imhotep was chief architect, physician, and philosopher to Pharaoh Djoser. He designed the Step Pyramid — the first large-scale stone construction in human history. He was also the world's first named physician, documenting medical practice in scrolls that influenced Egyptian medicine for 2,000 years. He was deified after death and worshipped as a god of medicine for 3,000 years. The Greeks renamed him Asclepius. History renamed the credit.
Why it matters: One man — architect, doctor, philosopher, engineer simultaneously. This is what the New Earth's integrated education system produces: people who are not one thing but many, whose knowledge serves the community in multiple dimensions at once.

c.2700 BCE
Merit-Ptah — the first named woman in the history of science
Kemet
▼
Chief physician to the Pharaoh — the first woman in recorded history known by name to have practised medicine. Her image in her son's tomb is inscribed "chief physician." This is 2,000 years before Hippocrates. Her existence tells you something profound about Kemet: a society governed by Ma'at — cosmic balance and truth — does not waste talent based on gender. Competence was the qualification.
Why it matters: The New Earth does not waste the gifts of half its people. The divine feminine is not separate from governance, medicine, or leadership. Merit-Ptah is the ancestral proof.

c.1550 BCE
The Ebers Papyrus — 842 medical remedies documented
Thebes, Kemet
▼
A 110-page scroll, 20 metres long, containing 842 medical remedies covering internal medicine, dermatology, gynaecology, dentistry, and psychiatry. Herbs documented include aloe vera, cannabis, myrrh, frankincense, garlic, castor oil, senna, henna, juniper, opium, and thyme. It documents the circulatory system with the heart at its centre — 3,500 years before William Harvey is credited with "discovering" it. Still in existence. Held at the University of Leipzig.
Why it matters: This is Dr Sebi's entire philosophy — plants as medicine, the body as a system to be understood not suppressed — documented 3,500 years ago. The knowledge was always there.

c.1600 BCE
Edwin Smith Papyrus — the world's oldest surgical textbook
Kemet
▼
48 cases of trauma documented with clinical precision — head injuries, spinal fractures, dislocations, tumours. First text in history to use the word "brain" and describe its function. Completely rational, evidence-based — no magic, only observation. Likely based on texts from the Old Kingdom, making it potentially 5,000 years old. Originally attributed to Imhotep. Acquired by Edwin Smith in 1862. Now held at the New York Academy of Medicine.
Why it matters: 5,000 years ago, African physicians were performing surgery with documented protocols. The "primitive" narrative is not just wrong — it is a deliberate inversion of the historical record.
Research: The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus Vol.1 — James Henry Breasted (Oriental Institute, University of Chicago)
814 BCE
Dido of Carthage founds the city — one of the ancient world's greatest trading powers
Carthage (modern Tunisia)
▼
Dido (also known as Elissa) was a Phoenician princess who fled a murderous brother and founded Carthage on the North African coast. The city became one of the Mediterranean's dominant powers, controlling trade routes across the western Mediterranean. Hannibal — one of history's greatest military commanders — was Carthaginian. Mago of Carthage wrote the world's first agricultural encyclopaedia. When Rome finally destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, it ordered Mago's work translated into Latin immediately — because even in destruction, they recognised the knowledge was too valuable to lose.
Why it matters: An African woman founded one of the ancient world's most powerful cities. The history of Africa before colonialism is a history of power, sovereignty, and genius — not absence.
Research: Carthage Must Be Destroyed — Richard Miles · Encyclopaedia Britannica on Carthage
c.100–940 CE
Kingdom of Aksum — Africa's first Christian empire and Red Sea trade power
Ethiopia & Eritrea
▼
One of the four great powers of the ancient world alongside Rome, Persia, and China — as described by the Persian prophet Mani in the 3rd century CE. Aksum controlled Red Sea trade routes between the Mediterranean, Arabia, and India. It produced its own currency — one of only five kingdoms in the ancient world to do so. It adopted Christianity in the 4th century CE, before most of Europe. Its currency was gold. Its architecture included the Aksumite stelae — granite towers up to 33 metres tall — some of which are still standing.
Why it matters: Africa was not on the periphery of the ancient world. It was at the centre of it — connected, trading, innovating, and governing sovereign states for millennia.
Research: The Kingdom of Aksum — Stuart Munro-Hay · Ancient Ethiopia — David Phillipson
600 CE – 1600 CE

Medieval Africa & the Islamic golden age

While Europe was in the Dark Ages, Africa was in a golden one. The Mali Empire, Timbuktu, the Songhai Empire, Great Zimbabwe, and the Swahili Coast were building civilisations of extraordinary sophistication.

989 CE
The University of Sankore founded in Timbuktu
Timbuktu, Mali
▼
Founded by Al-Qadi Aqib Ibn Mahmud — a judge and scholar. The university grew from a mosque into one of the world's most important centres of learning. At its peak: 25,000 students in a city of 100,000. Four degree levels, the highest taking 10 years to complete. Subjects: law, medicine, astronomy, mathematics, physics, chemistry, philosophy, geography, history, art, and practical trades. Oxford was founded 107 years later and had a fraction of the students. The intellectual freedom enjoyed in Western universities was directly inspired by institutions like Sankore.
Why it matters: The Kin-nect library, the study circles, the knowledge commons — this is what they are building toward. Timbuktu did it first and for centuries. We know it works.

1235 CE
Sundiata Keita founds the Mali Empire after the Battle of Kirina
West Africa
▼
Sundiata Keita — the "Lion King" — defeated Sumanguru Kante of the Sosso Empire at the Battle of Kirina and founded the Mali Empire. His story is preserved in the Sundiata Epic — one of the great oral epics of world literature, still performed by griots (oral historians) today. The Mali Empire at its peak covered an area larger than Western Europe. Its founding was not just military: Sundiata established a constitution called the Kouroukan Fouga, which guaranteed rights to women, prohibited slavery for certain groups, and enshrined environmental protection. Written down in the 1990s. Predates Magna Carta by 22 years.
Why it matters: The Kouroukan Fouga is a constitution from 1235 that included environmental protections and women's rights. Our ancestors had this figured out 800 years ago.

1324 CE
Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca — the wealthiest journey in history
Mali to Mecca
▼
Mansa Musa set out for Mecca with 60,000 people including 12,000 personal servants and a baggage train carrying tonnes of gold. He gave gold away freely along the route — to the poor, to foreign dignitaries, to mosques. The sheer quantity of gold he introduced into the economies of Egypt and the Middle East caused inflation that lasted twelve years. His generosity alone disrupted the global economy. He used his wealth on return to commission the Djinguereber Mosque, expand the universities, and import scholars from across the Islamic world.
Why it matters: The wealthiest person in recorded history was African. He used his wealth to build knowledge infrastructure. This is the model: wealth in service of community, not personal accumulation.

c.1100–1450 CE
Great Zimbabwe — the largest stone structure in sub-Saharan Africa
Zimbabwe
▼
A city of 18,000 people at its peak, governed by the Shona people. Its stone enclosures — built without mortar — include walls 11 metres high and 5 metres thick. At its centre, the Great Enclosure is the largest ancient structure in sub-Saharan Africa. It controlled the gold trade routes between the interior and the Swahili Coast ports. When European colonisers first encountered it in the 19th century, they refused to believe Africans had built it — attributing it to Phoenicians, Arabs, anyone but the Shona. The buildings were literally signed by their builders in the stone.
Why it matters: Natural building, community at scale, trade sovereignty — Great Zimbabwe had all of it. The country named itself after this place in 1980. The ancestors left their names in stone.

1375 CE
Ibn Khaldun writes the Muqaddimah — the world's first social science
North Africa (born in Tunisia)
▼
Ibn Khaldun's Muqaddimah (Introduction to History) is the world's first systematic work of sociology, economics, and political science. He developed theories of social cohesion (asabiyyah), the rise and fall of civilisations, economic cycles, and the relationship between climate and culture — 400 years before European scholars would attempt the same. His analysis of why civilisations collapse is more sophisticated than most modern political theory. Available in English translation. Mandatory reading for anyone building a community.
Why it matters: He identified exactly the patterns — internal cohesion, economic exploitation, corruption of leadership — that destroy communities. Understanding them is how you build one that lasts.

1600 CE – 1900 CE

Diaspora & resistance

The period of the transatlantic slave trade and its aftermath. Not a history of victimhood — a history of resistance, survival, and the extraordinary things built against every possible obstacle.

1600s–present
The Maroon communities — free cities in the mountains
Jamaica, Suriname, Brazil, USA
▼
Across the Caribbean and Americas, enslaved Africans escaped and built free communities — the Maroons of Jamaica, the Quilombos of Brazil (Palmares had 30,000 people and lasted 100 years), the Saramaka and Ndyuka of Suriname (still sovereign peoples today). They maintained African governance, spiritual practices, food systems, and languages. Queen Nanny defeated the British so decisively they signed a peace treaty in 1739. The Suriname Maroons signed a similar treaty in 1762. These were sovereign states built by people who had been enslaved.
Why it matters: Every argument that Black people "can't" build self-sufficient communities is answered by the Maroons. They built them with nothing, in hostile terrain, against an empire. We have more.

1739 CE
Treaty of Cudjoe — Britain legally recognises Maroon sovereignty
Jamaica
▼
After decades of guerrilla warfare in which the Jamaican Maroons — under Captain Cudjoe and the spiritual leadership of Queen Nanny — consistently defeated British regular forces, Britain signed a peace treaty. The treaty granted the Maroons land rights, freedom, and self-governance in perpetuity in exchange for not harbouring newly escaped enslaved people. It was a compromise. But it was also a British Empire legally acknowledging that a community of formerly enslaved Africans had defeated it. That has never been taught in British schools.
Why it matters: Diplomatic sovereignty is possible. The New Earth does not only build — it negotiates, strategically, from a position of demonstrated power.

1791–1804 CE
Haitian Revolution — the only successful slave revolt in history
Haiti (then Saint-Domingue)
▼
Enslaved people in Saint-Domingue (now Haiti) rose up in 1791, led by Toussaint L'Ouverture, Jean-Jacques Dessalines, and Henri Christophe. They defeated the armies of France, Britain, and Spain. Napoleon sent 86,000 soldiers — they were defeated. Haiti declared independence on January 1, 1804, becoming the first Black republic in the world and the second republic in the Western Hemisphere. France then forced Haiti to pay reparations for the "property" (enslaved people) that had freed itself — a debt not fully paid until 1947. Haiti has been economically strangled for its audacity ever since.
Why it matters: Haiti proved it was possible. The entire Western world spent the next two centuries making sure it paid a price for that proof — which tells you exactly how threatening it was.

1825–1857 CE
Seneca Village — a Black community where Central Park now stands
Manhattan, New York
▼
A 25-year-old shoeshiner named Andrew Williams paid $125 for three lots of land in upper Manhattan in 1825 — two years before slavery was abolished in New York State. Within 30 years: 50 homes, 3 churches, 2 schools, gardens, livestock, a natural spring, and a community that owned its land and therefore its right to vote. Demolished by the city in 1857 for Central Park. Residents called squatters. No photographs exist. The city changed the zoning laws to prevent rebuilding. Archaeologists confirmed the site in 2011.
Why it matters: The minimum viable community — land, water, food, school, church — can be built in 30 years from $125. And it will be destroyed if it is not legally protected. Both lessons are essential.

1865 CE onwards
The Freedom Colonies — hundreds of land-based communities built by freed people
American South
▼
Immediately after emancipation, formerly enslaved people pooled resources and bought land. In Texas alone, over 500 Freedom Colonies were established. They built churches first, schools second, farms third. Eatonville, Florida — founded 1887 — is the oldest continuously inhabited Black township in the United States. Most Freedom Colonies were eventually destroyed by land theft, highway construction, or economic strangulation. A few survive. Royal, Florida — under threat from turnpike extension today — is described as "one of the last remaining Black communities left largely intact since emancipation."
Why it matters: Formerly enslaved people with nothing immediately understood: land first. Church. School. Food. In that order. They were right then. They are right now.

1870 CE – 2000 CE

The modern era

Economic sovereignty built and destroyed. Communities burned, bulldozed, and erased by law. Independence won across the continent. The pattern repeats — and the lesson deepens: build wider, build legally protected, build networked.

1871 CE onwards
Jackson Ward — the first Black Wall Street, Richmond Virginia
Richmond, Virginia, USA
▼
35 years before Greenwood, Jackson Ward in Richmond was already being called "the Harlem of the South" and "the Black Wall Street." It was home to the first Black-owned life insurance company in America, and to Maggie Lena Walker — the first Black woman to charter and serve as president of a US bank. Walker said explicitly: "Let us put our money together; let us use our money; let us put our money out at usury among ourselves." Jackson Ward did not burn. It was slowly strangled — by redlining, economic isolation, and then literally cut in half by the construction of Interstate 95 in the 1950s. The highway was not routed through Jackson Ward by accident.
Why it matters: The weapon changed from mobs to planning departments. Highways replaced fire. The destruction of thriving Black communities through "urban renewal" killed more communities than racial violence. The New Earth must be legally protected against both.

1880s–1960s CE
Hayti District, Durham — home of the largest Black-owned business on Earth
Durham, North Carolina, USA
▼
Named deliberately after Haiti — the first Black republic — the Hayti District of Durham was anchored by Parrish Street, which became known as "Black Wall Street of the South." North Carolina Mutual Life Insurance, founded here, became the largest Black-owned business in the world at its peak. Durham's first Black physician established practice here in 1888. The Biltmore Hotel, the Regal Theater, hundreds of Black-owned businesses. The community was not destroyed by fire or mob — it was bulldozed for the construction of NC Highway 147. A thriving economic district turned into a road. The people were simply moved. Their wealth was not.
Why it matters: Hayti chose its name carefully. Haiti meant: we are free, we are sovereign, we build for ourselves. The highway meant: the state can always take what you build if it is not legally protected. Community Land Trusts exist precisely because of what happened to Hayti.

January 1923 CE

▼
