What was built before us

Our people built some of the greatest civilisations the world has ever known — before and after slavery. These are not distant history. They are the blueprints already in our lineage. Every single one shared the same core architecture. We build it again.

3100 BCE – 30 BCE · Over 3,000 years of continuous civilisation

Kemet — Ancient Egypt

"I have not done wrong. I have not stolen. I have not killed. I have not caused suffering. I have not destroyed food. I have not told lies." The 42 Negative Confessions — spoken before Ma'at at the moment of judgement. Not aspiration. Daily standard.
The founding principle of Kemet — Ma'at as the operating system of life

A civilisation built on Ma'at — cosmic order, truth, justice, and balance — as the operating system for every aspect of life. Not just a religious idea but the actual constitution of how government, economics, medicine, and education functioned. The pyramids were built by paid, fed workers. Every level of society understood their role in the collective prosperity.

Food & water
Nile irrigation systems — canals, shaduf water-lifts, seasonal flood management
Grain storehouses redistributed to the entire population — central food security
Organised farming calendar aligned to flooding, planting, and harvest seasons
Bread and beer as staple foods — mass-produced at state level
Medicine — the medical papyri
Ebers Papyrus (c.1550 BCE) — 842 remedies across 110 pages, 20 metres long. Covers internal medicine, dermatology, gynaecology, dentistry, and psychiatry. Herbs documented include: aloe vera (burns, ulcers, skin disease), basil (heart medicine), frankincense (infections, wounds), cannabis (pain, inflammation), myrrh (antiseptic), garlic (infections, parasites), juniper (diuretic), castor oil (laxative), fennel (digestive), senna (purgative), henna (skin), thyme, opium. Birth control documented: dates, acacia, and honey paste as pessary. Guinea worm treatment: wrap emerging worm around a stick and slowly pull — still the standard treatment 3,500 years later.
Edwin Smith Papyrus (c.1600 BCE) — the world's oldest surgical textbook. 48 trauma cases documented with scientific precision: head injuries, spinal fractures, dislocations, tumours, wounds. First text to use the word "brain." Rational, evidence-based — no magic, just observation and technique. Originally from the Old Kingdom (c.3000 BCE), making it potentially 5,000 years old.
Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus (c.1825 BCE) — the oldest known medical text in Egyptian history. 34 segments addressing women's health: gynaecology, fertility, pregnancy, contraception. Acknowledged that many conditions were systemic rather than local.
Specialised physicians — ophthalmologists, dentists, proctologists, surgeons all documented as distinct specialisations. Court physicians were among the most respected people in the kingdom.
Research: Herbal Medicine in Ancient Egypt — Lans et al., J. Ethnobiology & Ethnomedicine · The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus Vol.1 — James Henry Breasted (Oriental Institute, Chicago) · Ebers Papyrus — held at University of Leipzig Library
Education
Children with mothers until age 4 — motherhood honoured as first teacher
Age 7: 40-year education in temples — maths, astronomy, medicine, architecture, law
Teachers were priests AND scribes AND military — no separation of knowledge
Education was holistic — spiritual, scientific, practical, communal as one
Governance — how Ma'at was implemented
Ma'at as constitution — not a written code but a lived standard. Every official wore or displayed Ma'at's feather as their oath of office. The pharaoh was required to "offer Ma'at daily" — meaning their legitimacy depended on demonstrating justice and balance, not just holding power. A pharaoh who violated Ma'at lost divine favour and risked political collapse.
The Vizier — the pharaoh's chief administrator and supreme judge. Viziers were explicitly told: "Do not act willfully in cases where the law is unknown to you; acquaint yourself with it." Law required knowledge, not rank.
The courts — judges were called "priests of Ma'at." They operated on precedent and moral instruction, not written codes. The goal was always restoration of balance, not punishment. Restorative justice, 3,000 years before the term was coined.
In daily life — Ma'at was practical: accurate weights in the market, truth in disputes, care for family and community. An Egyptian merchant using false weights was not just breaking a rule — they were violating cosmic law and would face consequences in this life and the next.
The 42 Negative Confessions — the standard against which every person was judged at death. Not "I tried to be good" — "I have not stolen. I have not lied. I have not caused suffering." A society that holds its people to that standard, in their daily actions, is a different kind of society.
Research: The Role of Ma'at in Ancient Egyptian Law — Brewminate.com · Ma'at: The Moral Ideal in Ancient Egypt — Maulana Karenga · The Egyptian Book of the Dead — E.A. Wallis Budge (trans.)
Architecture & building
Mudbrick homes for all — natural material, cool in heat, locally made
Pyramids and temples built by paid, organised, fed workers
Stone carving, mathematics, and engineering mastered over centuries
Buildings aligned to stars and seasons — structure in harmony with cosmos
Economy
State-organised redistribution — goods not hoarded but circulated
Workers paid in grain, oil, cloth — real goods, not symbolic money
Trade routes to Nubia, Punt, Mediterranean — external wealth without dependency
Temples as economic hubs — stored goods, employed people, fed communities
What we take from Kemet
Ma'at as governance — the principle that every individual action either upholds or disrupts the collective harmony. The integration of education: spiritual, scientific, practical, and communal taught as one body of knowledge. The temple as the centre of community life — not just worship but food storage, medicine, education, and economics. Mudbrick homes built from the land itself. Workers fed and paid in real goods.
989 CE – present · The Mali Empire at its peak: 1235–1600 CE

Mali Empire & Timbuktu

Books were the most valuable commodity in Timbuktu. Merchants who arrived to trade gold and salt left having spent more on manuscripts than on any other goods. Knowledge was the founding principle of this civilisation — not wealth, not military power. Knowledge first. Everything else followed.
The founding principle of Timbuktu

Mansa Musa's Mali Empire was simultaneously the wealthiest and the most educated civilisation on Earth. Timbuktu was not a city of gold — it was a city of knowledge. A single pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324 distributed so much gold it caused inflation across North Africa and the Middle East for twelve years. He used that wealth to build universities. In 1324, Oxford had existed for 200 years. Sankore had been running for over 300.

What they studied — select a subject
LAW — MANDATORY STUDY
Islamic law, jurisprudence & the science of justice
Law was the core of the Sankore curriculum — not as a set of rules to memorise, but as a living discipline of ethical reasoning. Students studied the Maliki school of jurisprudence, learning how to apply divine principles to real disputes: inheritance, contracts, property, marriage, governance, and criminal justice. Judges in Timbuktu were also scholars — their authority came from their depth of knowledge, not their rank. The concept that law and scholarship are inseparable is one of the most important things Timbuktu has to teach. A community that cannot reason through its own disputes from its own principles is not yet sovereign.
Research: The Fate of the Timbuktu Manuscripts — Shamil Jeppie & Souleymane Bachir Diagne (eds.) · Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire — John Hunwick · Al-Furqan Islamic Heritage Foundation catalogue (digitallibrary.al-furqan.com)
The university structure
Founded 989 CE — older than Oxford (1096) by over 100 years
Three institutions: Sankore, Djinguereber, Sidi Yahia — each independent, each run by a master scholar
25,000 students in a city of 100,000 — one in four people was a student
No central administration — each scholar ran their own college from their home or mosque courtyard
Four degree levels, the highest equivalent to a PhD — taking 10 years minimum to complete
Graduates received cloth, salt, livestock, and money — real goods, not symbolic certificates
The manuscripts — what they are
Estimated 700,000+ manuscripts across Timbuktu — written on paper, goat skin, sheep skin, fish skin
Topics: medicine, astronomy, mathematics, law, theology, philosophy, geography, trade, music, poetry, history, magic
Written primarily in Arabic — the lingua franca of scholarship — with some in Songhay and Fulani
Held in family libraries for generations — passed down with the oath "I will protect this library for as long as I live"
In 2012, jihadist forces occupied Timbuktu — Dr Abdel Kader Haidara organised the secret evacuation of 350,000 manuscripts to Bamako in the south
4,203 manuscripts were burned before evacuation was complete

Economy
Gold and salt trade — controlled trans-Saharan routes between West and North Africa
Books were more valuable than gold at market — a scholar's library was their greatest asset
Taxation used to build public infrastructure, not accumulate personal wealth
Mansa Musa's 1324 pilgrimage: 60,000 people, 12,000 slaves carrying gold, inflation for 12 years
Architecture
Djinguereber Mosque — commissioned 1327 by Mansa Musa, still standing today
Built from mud brick and wood — Sudano-Sahelian style, thermally efficient in desert heat
Annual replastering festival — the entire community maintains the buildings together each year
Cities designed around knowledge institutions — libraries before palaces, schools before markets
What we take from Timbuktu
Knowledge is the most valuable thing a community can produce and protect. Books were worth more than gold. The university had no central administration — it was a network of independent scholars, each running their own school. That is the model: distributed, decentralised, each node holding its own knowledge. The families who hid manuscripts in the desert and passed down the oath "I will protect this library for as long as I live" — that is the spirit of the New Earth's knowledge keeper. Document everything. The Timbuktu manuscripts were almost lost forever. Kin-nect exists so that what is known now cannot be erased.

1906 – 1921 · Post-slavery Black economic sovereignty on American soil

Black Wall Street — Greenwood District

The most complete modern proof of what a self-sufficient Black community looks like. Built post-slavery, under Jim Crow, against every legal and social barrier — and it thrived so powerfully it was destroyed by the state. The dollar circulated 19 to 36 times before leaving the community. Today the Black dollar circulates for 6 hours.

"When O.W. Gurley purchased over 40 acres of land, he committed to selling it to Black people only. His investments became an economic model of community determination, economic power, and resilience."
Black Wall Street — the founding principle
What was built
2 newspapers — information controlled by the community
2 schools, banks, hospitals, and law firms
Hotels, theatres, grocery stores, pharmacies, barber shops
Over 300 businesses in a 35-block area
Every service a community needs — inside the community
The economic model
Dollar circulated 19–36 times before leaving — multiplied internal wealth
Community deliberately chose to spend with each other first
Credit and banking within — no reliance on external financial institutions
Professional class resident — doctors, lawyers, teachers all inside
Churches as community anchors — spiritual and social centre
Why it was destroyed — June 1, 1921
A white mob, aided by local police, the Oklahoma National Guard, and private aircraft, burned 35 blocks to the ground. 300+ residents killed. 10,000 made homeless. $150 million in today's money destroyed overnight. Then zoning laws were changed to prevent rebuilding. The message was explicit: a fully self-sufficient Black community is an existential threat to any system that profits from Black dependency. You do not destroy what does not threaten you.
What we take from Black Wall Street
The circulating dollar is the single most replicable lesson. Every time you spend inside the community, it multiplies. Every time it leaves, it's gone. The complete institution model: every service your community needs must exist inside it. Conscious economic loyalty: Greenwood didn't happen by accident — people chose each other deliberately. And the protection lesson: the New Earth must be distributed, legally protected, and networked so no single act can destroy the whole.
1825 – 1857 · Manhattan's first significant Black property-owning community

Seneca Village — Central Park

Andrew Williams was 25 years old, a shoeshiner. In September 1825 he paid $125 for three lots of land in upper Manhattan. Within weeks, a church bought adjacent land. Within years, a village existed. The founding principle: begin before you are ready, with less than you think you need.
The founding principle of Seneca Village — one person, one act, one beginning

A 25-year-old shoeshiner named Andrew Williams bought the first three lots for $125 in 1825 — two years before slavery was even abolished in New York State. Within weeks, a church had purchased land. Within years, a village existed. By 1855: 50 homes, 3 churches, 2 schools, gardens, livestock, and a natural spring. This is how fast a community can be built when people move with intention.

What was actually built
50 homes — mostly two-storey, stone foundation walls confirmed by archaeologists
3 churches — community spiritual and social centres
2 schools — including one for Black children excluded from city schools
2 cemeteries — community burial on their own land
Streets, barns, gardens, and livestock pens
How they lived
Gardens and livestock — partial food sovereignty from day one
Hudson River fishing — free protein from the surrounding land
Tanner's Spring — natural water source on the land itself
Children attended school — education a community priority
Multiracial — Black, Irish, and German families living side by side
Why it was destroyed — 1857
The city wanted the land for Central Park. The community was labelled a "shantytown" and residents were called "squatters and vagabonds" in the press — the same language used to justify displacement throughout history. Eminent domain was used. Residents fought for two years. Police removed those who resisted by force. When they left, losing land meant losing the right to vote. The destruction erased a community and its political power simultaneously. No photographs of Seneca Village exist.
What we take from Seneca Village
Land + water source + food growing + school + church is the complete minimum viable community. It can be built faster than you think — from zero to 50 homes and three churches in 30 years, starting with one shoeshiner's $125. Community Land Trusts exist precisely to prevent eminent domain — the land held collectively, so no individual can be bought out and no single act can dispossess everyone. This time, the structure protects the vision.
1600s – present · Free African sovereign communities built inside slave territories

Maroon communities

Queen Nanny. Leader of the Windward Maroons. Military commander. Spiritual guide. Obeah woman. She defeated the British Empire so decisively that it signed a peace treaty with her in 1739, legally recognising the sovereignty of her people. The founding principle: freedom is not given. It is taken, held, and defended — spiritually, physically, and diplomatically.
The founding principle of the Maroon communities — sovereignty is not negotiated, it is established

The most radical proof of all. People who escaped slavery — with nothing — built free communities in hostile terrain and held them for centuries. A sovereign people, negotiating as equals with a colonial power.

What was built
Mountain and forest communities — difficult terrain was the first defence
Food growing in hidden clearings — self-sufficient from the first days
Knowledge of local plants for medicine and food — the critical survival skill
Networks of trails and escape routes known only to community members
African traditional governance maintained — councils of elders
Diplomatic achievement
1739 Treaty of Cudjoe — British legally recognised Maroon sovereignty
Cockpit Country granted to the Maroons — land sovereignty in perpetuity
Suriname Maroons — 6 sovereign peoples still existing today
Quilombo dos Palmares in Brazil — 30,000 people, lasted 100 years
African spiritual traditions preserved through generations of pressure
What we take from the Maroons
Freedom is always possible, even from the most extreme oppression. The terrain matters — build where you have natural protection. African spiritual practice and governance survived centuries because it was embedded in daily life, not kept separate. Guerrilla resilience: when you can't win by force, you win by endurance, terrain, and community cohesion. Queen Nanny knew when to fight and when to negotiate. The New Earth needs both capacities.

This is the upgrade the ancestors didn't have the technology to build. We do.
Part Two

The spiritual & inner foundation

Before a single brick is laid or a seed planted, the inner ground must be prepared. Every great civilisation — Kemet, Mali, the Maroons — built its physical world on a spiritual operating system. The New Earth is no different.

What is a spiritual foundation — really?

Not religion. Not performance. The operating system beneath everything. The thing that answers: Why do we build this? What holds us together when it gets hard? What do we owe each other? What do we owe those who come after?

In Kemet, Ma'at was not a belief system people chose to adopt. It was the air. It governed the stars, the floods, the courts, the medicine, the buildings, the harvest, and the way people greeted each other. There was no separation between spiritual life and daily life. The New Earth cannot have that separation either.
Principle of integration — Kemetic civilisation
What the spiritual foundation is not
A Sunday practice separate from the rest of life
A belief system that must be agreed on by everyone
A hierarchy of who is more spiritual than others
Performances of piety or rituals without inner meaning
Something that can be built later, once everything else is in place
What the spiritual foundation actually is
The shared values that everyone acts from, even in disagreement
The practices that keep people connected to themselves and each other
The memory of where we come from — ancestors, land, lineage
The understanding that we build for the seventh generation
The thing that makes every task — farming, healing, building — sacred
The three layers
Every strong spiritual foundation operates at three levels: Personal — the individual's inner life, healing, and relationship to the Most High. Relational — how people treat each other, resolve conflict, and hold each other accountable. Cosmic — the community's relationship to land, ancestors, seasons, and the divine order. All three must be built. Inner first, then relational, then cosmic.

Truth — the first principle
The ability to distinguish the real from the unreal. Not just intellectual honesty, but the deeper commitment to see things as they are — in yourself, in your community, in the world. Truth includes the recognition that all living beings are sacred. You cannot build on a lie, a pretence, or a performance. The New Earth begins with people willing to see clearly.
Practice: Before any community decision — "Are we telling the truth about what is actually happening here?" Personally: morning journaling to track the gap between who you are and who you are becoming.
Ma'at as community constitution
In Kemet, these principles weren't aspirational — they were operational. Every court, every dispute, every agricultural decision was measured against Ma'at. The community charter of the New Earth should be written in Ma'at's language: not "our rules" but "what we align with." Rules can be broken. Cosmic principles can only be violated — and the consequences come from the universe itself.

Ubuntu — I am because we are

The Southern African philosophical foundation of collective identity. Not a belief system — a description of reality. You did not become who you are alone. Your community made you. Which means: building the community is building yourself.

"A person is a person through other people. My humanity is caught up, is inextricably bound up, in yours. When I dehumanise you, I dehumanise myself."
Archbishop Desmond Tutu — on Ubuntu
What Ubuntu teaches about building
You cannot build the New Earth alone — the attempt proves you haven't understood Ubuntu
The community's wellbeing is your personal spiritual practice
Conflict in the community is information — it shows where Ubuntu is blocked
Children are raised by all — parenting is a collective spiritual act
Elders are not optional extras — they are the memory of the collective
Ubuntu in practice
Food is shared before any individual eats — Ubuntu at the table
Decisions wait for the quietest voice to be heard
When one is in crisis, the community responds — not charities, not services
Restorative justice: not "how do we punish?" but "how do we heal?"
Celebration is collective — no one's victory belongs to them alone
The individualism trap
The greatest weapon the system used against Black communities was individualism — the idea that your success is yours alone, and others' failures are theirs alone. Ubuntu is the direct antidote. But it must be consciously chosen, because the dominant culture programmes the opposite from birth. The inner work of Ubuntu is catching yourself thinking "mine" when the truth is "ours."
Ubuntu and the New Earth economy
Ubuntu is not just a spiritual principle — it is an economic one. Cooperative land ownership is Ubuntu in practice. Sharing seeds is Ubuntu. The circulating dollar of Black Wall Street was Ubuntu. When you understand that your neighbour's prosperity is your prosperity, you stop competing and start compounding. This is why Greenwood's dollar circulated 19–36 times — people were practising Ubuntu economically without necessarily naming it.

Inner work — the work that must come first

You cannot build a New Earth from an unexamined self. Every unhealed wound you carry will appear in the community you try to build. The inner work is not a detour from the mission — it is the mission's foundation.

🪞
Know what you are building from
Fear or love? Trauma or vision? The same action — building a community, growing food, healing others — can be driven by two completely different energies. One builds something that lasts. The other collapses when the fear is resolved or the wound is triggered. Before you build anything outside, spend time with this question: what am I running from, and what am I building toward?
Journaling practice — weekly minimum
🧬
Ancestral healing — releasing what was carried
Trauma passes through the bloodline. The fear, the shame, the rage, the grief of your ancestors lives in your nervous system. You did not choose it. But it is yours to resolve. Ancestral healing is not about dwelling in pain — it is about consciously completing what previous generations could not. When you heal, you heal backwards and forwards simultaneously.
Ancestral altar practice · guided sessions with an elder or healer
🫀
Emotional sovereignty
You cannot govern a community if you cannot govern yourself. Emotional sovereignty means knowing what you feel, why you feel it, and choosing how to respond rather than react. The person who can stay present and calm when the community is in conflict is worth ten people who can build with their hands. Develop this capacity before you need it.
Breathwork · meditation · somatic practice · therapeutic support
🔥
Clarify your purpose — your specific gift
What did you come here to do? Not in vague terms — specifically. Your gift is not your job title. It is the thing you cannot help but do, the thing that makes you feel most alive and most useful. When you know this, every decision — who to align with, what to build, what to sacrifice — becomes clearer.
Vision work · mentorship · contemplative retreat
🌙
Silence and stillness as discipline
The system is designed to keep you in perpetual noise — phones, news, outrage, urgency. Silence is radical. In silence you hear what you actually think, feel what you actually feel, and receive what the Most High is actually trying to give you. Every great teacher across every tradition built silence into their daily practice. You cannot receive guidance if you are always broadcasting.
Minimum 20 minutes daily — before the phone, before the world

Building it collectively

A community with shared spiritual practice is orders of magnitude more resilient than one held together by shared ideology alone. Practice — the things you do together repeatedly — is what builds the felt sense of being one body.

🌅
Morning gathering — the daily anchor
Even 15 minutes. Greeting the day together. A moment of intention, gratitude, and alignment before the day's work begins. This is not a meeting — it has no agenda. It is a practice of recognising each other as part of the same body. In Kemet, workers began each day with an offering to Ma'at. The Maroons began each day with ancestral acknowledgement.
Daily — non-negotiable
🔵
Community council circle — the weekly truth-telling
A structured time for the community to speak honestly about what is working and what is not. Each person speaks without interruption. No one responds until all have spoken. The circle is the form because the circle has no head. This is where Ubuntu becomes operational — where "I am because we are" is tested and proven in real time.
Weekly — held in a circle, never around a table
🥁
Music, movement, and celebration
Not entertainment — medicine. Drumming, singing, dance, and shared movement regulate the nervous system, synchronise the community, and create the felt experience of being one body. This is why every African civilisation had music at the centre of communal life. Schedule celebration like you schedule work — because it is work. It is the work of staying human.
Weekly minimum · monthly full celebration
🕯
Ancestral remembrance — the monthly honouring
Light a candle. Speak the names. Pour libation. Tell the stories. When you know who came before you, and what they endured to get you here, you make different decisions. You build differently. You treat the land differently. The seventh-generation principle begins with honouring the seven generations behind you.
Monthly new or full moon · births, deaths, seasonal transitions
🌍
Land ceremony — the seasonal alignment
Mark the solstices, equinoxes, new moons, and harvest moments. The land is not your resource — it is your partner. Ceremony at seasonal transitions is the practice of remembering this. It prevents the extractive relationship that destroys communities and environments simultaneously. Kemet's entire agricultural calendar was organised around ceremony.
Four seasonal ceremonies · aligned to equinoxes and solstices
📖
Study circle — the weekly deep knowing
Reading, discussing, debating — together. History, science, philosophy, medicine, governance. What Timbuktu built was not just a physical university — it was a culture where study was considered a spiritual act. The community that studies together develops a shared intellectual and spiritual vocabulary.
Weekly · rotate who leads · read primary texts, not just summaries
🤲
Conflict as ceremony — restorative justice
Every community has conflict. The question is not how to prevent it but how to metabolise it. Restorative justice asks: what harm was done, what does the harmed person need, and how does the community heal? Not punishment — restoration. When conflict is held as ceremony — a sacred process of truth-telling and repair — it strengthens the community rather than fracturing it.
As needed — but the process must be designed in advance, in peace

The daily & weekly rhythm

Our History

Integral figures, inventors & scientists

This is not a history of suffering. This is a history of genius, creation, discovery, and resilience. Every person below changed the world. Most were never taught in school. Tap any card to go deeper.

The ancient world was shaped by African minds. These are not myths — they are documented historical figures whose contributions were later attributed to others, renamed, or simply erased.

c.2650 BCE · Kemet
Imhotep
Architect · Physician · Engineer · First named genius in recorded history
Imhotep designed the Step Pyramid of Djoser at Saqqara — the world's first large-scale stone structure. He was also chief physician to Pharaoh Djoser, an architect, astronomer, and philosopher. Deified after his death, he was worshipped as a god of medicine for 3,000 years. The Greeks later renamed him Asclepius and made him their own god of medicine. Hippocrates — credited as the father of medicine — was writing 2,000 years after Imhotep had already documented medicine systematically. Research: Imhotep the African — Molefi Kete Asante
c.2350 BCE · Kemet
Amenhotep, Son of Hapu
Architect · Philosopher · Healer · Second most revered sage of ancient Egypt
Chief architect to Pharaoh Amenhotep III, he oversaw the construction of the Colossi of Memnon and the Luxor temple complex. He was one of only two commoners to be deified after death. His philosophical writings influenced Egyptian thought for centuries. A direct ancestor of the tradition Imhotep began — genius recognised and honoured within a civilisation that understood what genius was for. Research: Ancient Egyptian Science — Marshall Clagett (3 volumes)
c.1550 BCE · Kemet
Merit-Ptah
Physician · First named woman in the history of science
Chief physician to the Pharaoh around 2700 BCE — the first woman in history known by name to have practised medicine. Her image was placed in her son's tomb with the inscription "chief physician." Predates Hippocrates by over 2,000 years. The fact that a woman was chief physician tells you something important about how Kemet actually worked: Ma'at did not discriminate by sex. Competence was the standard. Research: Women in Science — H.J. Mozans · Ancient Egyptian Medical Papyri
c.1350 BCE · Kemet
Nefertiti
Queen · Co-ruler · Political architect of the Amarna period
Wife of Pharaoh Akhenaten, Nefertiti was likely the most powerful woman in ancient Egypt. Evidence suggests she may have ruled as Pharaoh herself after her husband's death under the name Neferneferuaten. She co-authored one of history's most radical political experiments: the shift to monotheism under Aten. Whether that experiment was visionary or destructive is debated — but her power and influence are not. Research: Nefertiti: Egypt's Sun Queen — Joyce Tyldesley
c.1700s BCE · Kemet & Nubia
Ahmose-Nefertari
Queen · Founder of a religious order · Patron of artisans
Queen of Egypt during the 18th Dynasty and one of the most venerated women in Egyptian history. Deified after her death, she was worshipped for over 500 years — longer than most pharaohs. She founded and led a religious order, negotiated with the pharaoh for rights for the priesthood, and was patron of the craftsmen who built the royal tombs. Depicted with black skin — a mark of divinity and fertility in Egyptian art. Research: The Oxford History of Ancient Egypt — Ian Shaw (ed.)
c.51 BCE · Kemet
Cleopatra VII
Pharaoh · Linguist · Political strategist · Last ruler of independent Egypt
The last active ruler of the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Egypt. Spoke nine languages — the only Ptolemaic ruler to learn Egyptian. A trained mathematician and philosopher. She was not a passive figure manipulated by Roman men — she was the one manipulating Rome, using Caesar and Antony as political tools to maintain Egyptian independence for as long as possible. When she failed, she chose death over captivity and a Roman triumph. Research: Cleopatra: A Life — Stacy Schiff
c.400 BCE · Carthage (modern Tunisia)
Mago of Carthage
Agricultural scientist · Author of the world's first agricultural encyclopaedia
Mago wrote a 28-volume encyclopaedia on agriculture — so highly regarded that when Rome destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, the Roman Senate ordered Mago's work to be translated into Latin and distributed across the empire. It was considered the foundational text of European agriculture for centuries. An African scientist whose work fed Rome. Research: Roman Agriculture — K.D. White · Encyclopaedia Britannica entry on Mago
c.1312 CE · Mali Empire
Mansa Musa I
Emperor · Patron of learning · Wealthiest individual in recorded history
Emperor of the Mali Empire from 1312 to 1337. His 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca — with 60,000 people and tonnes of gold — caused inflation across North Africa and the Middle East for twelve years. He used his wealth to build universities, import scholars, and expand the libraries of Timbuktu. His empire spanned modern-day Mali, Senegal, Gambia, Guinea, Niger, Nigeria, Chad, and Mauritania. Some historians estimate his wealth at $400 billion in today's terms. Research: Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire — John Hunwick

Black scientists and inventors who changed the world — most without credit, many without recognition. These are the people who built modernity.

1731–1806 · Maryland, USA
Benjamin Banneker
Astronomer · Mathematician · Surveyor · Published scientist
Self-taught astronomer and mathematician who published almanacs from 1792 to 1797, containing astronomical calculations, tide tables, and medical remedies. He wrote to Thomas Jefferson challenging the hypocrisy of slavery in a nation claiming liberty. He helped survey the boundaries of Washington DC. Jefferson owned enslaved people and claimed Africans were intellectually inferior — Banneker's work was the direct refutation. Research: The Life of Benjamin Banneker — Silvio Bedini
1807–1893 · Maryland, USA
Lewis Howard Latimer
Inventor · Engineer · Draftsman for Edison and Bell
Latimer invented the carbon filament for the light bulb — making Edison's invention practical and affordable for mass use. Without Latimer's filament, electric light would have remained a laboratory curiosity. He also drafted the patent drawings for Alexander Graham Bell's telephone. He was the only Black member of Edison's elite team of inventors. His name appears on none of the famous patents. Research: Lewis Howard Latimer — Winifred Norman & Lily Patterson
1864–1943 · Missouri, USA
George Washington Carver
Agricultural scientist · Botanist · Inventor of 300+ peanut products
Developed over 300 products from peanuts and 100 from sweet potatoes — dyes, plastics, fuel, cosmetics, medicines — as part of a deliberate strategy to free Black Southern farmers from dependency on cotton. His agricultural science regenerated depleted Southern soil. He turned down a $100,000 salary from Thomas Edison, choosing to remain at Tuskegee. He donated his life savings to a foundation for agricultural research. Research: George Washington Carver: An American Biography — Rackham Holt
1893–1958 · Oklahoma, USA
Garrett Morgan
Inventor · Traffic signal · Gas mask · Safety hood
Invented the three-position traffic signal — the model used worldwide today. Invented the safety hood (precursor to the gas mask) and personally used it to rescue workers trapped in a tunnel under Lake Erie in 1916. Sold his traffic signal patent to General Electric for $40,000. When his identity became known after the tunnel rescue, some Southern cities cancelled their orders for his safety hood. Research: US Patent Office records — Patent No. 1,475,024 (traffic signal)
1918–2020 · Virginia, USA
Katherine Johnson
Mathematician · NASA · Calculated trajectories for moon landing
Her orbital mechanics calculations were critical to the success of the first US crewed spaceflights. John Glenn refused to fly unless Katherine Johnson personally verified the computer's calculations. She calculated the trajectory for Apollo 11 — the moon landing. She worked at NASA for 35 years, initially in a segregated computing pool. She received the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2015. Research: Hidden Figures — Margot Lee Shetterly
1891–1923 · Texas, USA
Bessie Coleman
Aviator · First Black woman to hold a pilot's licence
Denied flight training in the US because she was Black and a woman, she taught herself French, moved to France, and earned her international pilot's licence in 1921 — the first Black woman anywhere in the world to do so. She returned to the US and performed aerial stunts to raise money to open a flight school for Black Americans. She died in a plane crash at 34, before the school was built. It was built anyway, by her admirers. Research: Queen Bess: Daredevil Aviator — Doris L. Rich
1875–1965 · USA & Caribbean
Elijah McCoy
Mechanical engineer · Inventor · "The Real McCoy"
Invented automatic lubrication devices for steam engines — allowing trains to run without stopping to be oiled manually, transforming rail travel efficiency. He held 57 patents. Engineers specifying his devices over inferior imitations coined the phrase "the real McCoy." Born in Canada to formerly enslaved parents who escaped via the Underground Railroad, he could only find work as a fireman despite his engineering degree. Research: US Patent records · Black Inventors in the Age of Segregation — Rayvon Fouché
1916–2003 · Alabama, USA
Dr Percy Julian
Chemist · Synthesised cortisone · Pioneer of steroid chemistry
Synthesised physostigmine from the Calabar bean, pioneering the treatment of glaucoma. Later synthesised cortisone from soya beans — making it affordable for the first time and transforming treatment of arthritis and inflammatory diseases. He held over 130 chemical patents. His home was firebombed twice after he moved to a white Chicago suburb. He continued working. Research: Forgotten Genius — PBS documentary · Journal of Chemical Education

The healers, philosophers, and thinkers who gave us the frameworks to understand ourselves and the world. These are the ones whose ideas built civilisations and sustained communities through the unsurvivable.

1905–1988 · Honduras & New York
Dr Alfredo Darrington Bowman — Dr Sebi
Herbalist · Healer · Nutritionist · Alkaline medicine pioneer
Self-educated healer who developed an alkaline, plant-based approach to healing that claimed to address the cellular causes of disease rather than its symptoms. He was arrested by the New York Attorney General after advertising cures for AIDS and other diseases — and was acquitted after producing over 70 witnesses who testified he had healed them. His approach: the body is electrical, disease thrives in acid, alkaline plants restore the body's natural state. His influence runs through every serious conversation about natural healing in the Black diaspora. Research: Sebi's Natural Vegetation Cell Food — Dr Sebi · numerous lectures on YouTube
1918–1963 · Jamaica & USA
Claudia Jones
Political philosopher · Activist · Founded the Notting Hill Carnival
Trinidadian-born communist, feminist, and Black liberation thinker. Deported from the US, she came to Britain where she founded the West Indian Gazette — the UK's first major Black newspaper — and created the Notting Hill Carnival in 1959 in response to the race riots, as an act of cultural resistance and community healing. She theorised the "triple oppression" of Black women — by race, class, and sex — decades before "intersectionality" was named. Research: Claudia Jones: Beyond Containment — Carole Boyce Davies
1887–1940 · Jamaica & USA
Marcus Mosiah Garvey
Political philosopher · Pan-Africanist · Founded the UNIA
Founded the Universal Negro Improvement Association — the largest mass movement in Black history with over 6 million members at its peak. Organised the Black Star Line shipping company — a Black-owned transatlantic fleet. His philosophy: Africa for Africans, economic self-determination, and racial pride as a prerequisite for political freedom. He was prosecuted by the US government, imprisoned, and deported. His influence on Malcolm X, Martin Luther King, Nelson Mandela, and the Rastafari movement is direct and documented. Research: The Philosophy and Opinions of Marcus Garvey — Amy Jacques Garvey (ed.)
1923–2005 · Senegal
Cheikh Anta Diop
Historian · Anthropologist · Physicist · Proved Ancient Egypt was Black African
Produced the first rigorous scientific and historical evidence that ancient Egyptian civilisation was Black African — using melanin testing of mummies, linguistic analysis, and cultural comparison. His doctoral thesis was rejected twice by the French university system before being accepted. He went on to become a nuclear physicist, a senator in Senegal, and a UNESCO-recognised scholar. His work is the academic foundation for everything in the Kemet section of this blueprint. Research: The African Origin of Civilisation: Myth or Reality — Cheikh Anta Diop · Civilisation or Barbarism — Cheikh Anta Diop
1915–2005 · Barbados & UK
Dr Ivan Van Sertima
Historian · Linguist · Proved African contact with the Americas before Columbus
Author of They Came Before Columbus — the most rigorous academic case for African presence in the Americas pre-1492, using botanical evidence, skeletal analysis, linguistic traces, and the Olmec colossal heads (which bear unmistakably African features). A linguist and literary critic as well as a historian, he testified before a US Congressional committee on the rewriting of history. Research: They Came Before Columbus — Ivan Van Sertima · African Presence in Early America — Ivan Van Sertima (ed.)
1867–1963 · USA
W.E.B. Du Bois
Sociologist · Historian · Co-founder of the NAACP · First Black Harvard PhD
First Black American to receive a PhD from Harvard. His concept of "double consciousness" — the sense of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others — remains one of the most precise descriptions of the psychological experience of Blackness in a white world. His work The Souls of Black Folk is one of the most important books ever written. He co-founded the NAACP at 41, organised multiple Pan-African Congresses, and moved to Ghana at 93. Research: The Souls of Black Folk — W.E.B. Du Bois · Black Reconstruction in America — W.E.B. Du Bois

The people who built institutions, led movements, and created the structures our communities depended on — often against every obstacle the system could place in front of them.

c.1686–1733 · Jamaica
Queen Nanny of the Maroons
Military commander · Spiritual leader · National hero of Jamaica
Leader of the Windward Maroons of Jamaica. Escaped slavery and built a free community in the Blue Mountains. Led guerrilla warfare against the British so successfully that they signed a peace treaty in 1739, legally recognising Maroon sovereignty over Cockpit Country. She was an Obeah woman — her spiritual authority was inseparable from her military authority. She is one of only seven Jamaican National Heroes and the only woman among them. Her face is on the Jamaican $500 bill. Research: Queen of the Mountain — Werner Zips
1743–1803 · Haiti
Toussaint L'Ouverture
General · Revolutionary · Architect of Haitian independence
Born into slavery, became the leader of the Haitian Revolution — the only successful slave revolt in history that established a sovereign nation. He defeated the armies of three empires: France, Britain, and Spain. Napoleon sent 86,000 soldiers to retake Haiti. They were defeated. Toussaint was captured by treachery, imprisoned in France, and died in a cold mountain prison in 1803 — but Haiti declared independence six months later. He proved that enslaved people could defeat the most powerful military forces on Earth. Research: The Black Jacobins — C.L.R. James
1820–1913 · Maryland, USA
Harriet Tubman
Freedom fighter · Union spy · Conductor of the Underground Railroad
Escaped slavery and returned South thirteen times, leading approximately 70 people to freedom via the Underground Railroad. She never lost a passenger. She served as a spy and scout for the Union Army during the Civil War, leading the Combahee River Raid that freed over 700 enslaved people — the first military operation in US history planned and led by a woman. She suffered a traumatic brain injury as a child (from being hit with a heavy metal weight by an overseer) and experienced visions she attributed to God. She followed them. Research: Bound for the Promised Land — Kate Clifford Larson
1867–1919 · Durham, USA
Maggie Lena Walker
Banker · First Black woman to found and lead a US bank
Founded the St Luke Penny Savings Bank in Richmond, Virginia in 1903 — becoming the first Black woman to charter and serve as president of a bank in the United States. She built it from $31,500 in deposits to over $645,000 in assets. She said: "Let us put our money together; let us use our money; let us put our money out at usury among ourselves." This is the Black Wall Street principle stated explicitly. She built a department store, a newspaper, and an insurance company — all owned and operated by the Black community. Research: Maggie Lena Walker — Gertrude Marlowe
1875–1938 · Oklahoma, USA
O.W. Gurley
Educator · Entrepreneur · Architect of Black Wall Street
Moved to Tulsa in 1906 with $150 and bought 40 acres of land — committing to sell only to Black people. He built the first building in what became the Greenwood District and rented rooms to Black newcomers at affordable rates. A former educator and school principal, he understood that community required both economic infrastructure and cultural investment. Without Gurley's founding decision, Black Wall Street does not exist. His home was destroyed in the 1921 massacre. He was never fully compensated. Research: The Burning: Massacre, Destruction, and the Tulsa Race Riot of 1921 — Tim Madigan
1918–1987 · Jamaica & UK
Sam Selvon
Author · First major chronicler of Black British life
Arrived in Britain in 1950 on the same ship as George Lamming. His 1956 novel The Lonely Londoners was the first major literary account of the Windrush generation — their hopes, humiliations, humour, and survival in a hostile Britain. Written in Trinidadian dialect, it refused to translate itself for a white audience. He gave the community language for its own experience. Research: The Lonely Londoners — Sam Selvon · Moses Ascending — Sam Selvon

Culture is the soul of a civilisation. These are the people who gave us the words, music, images, and stories that kept us human, kept us whole, and told the world who we were — on our own terms.

1928–2014 · USA
Maya Angelou
Poet · Author · Activist · "I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings"
Her 1969 autobiography I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings was the first non-fiction bestseller by a Black woman in America. She worked with Malcolm X and Martin Luther King. She wrote the poem for Bill Clinton's inauguration. She said: "You may not control all the events that happen to you, but you can decide not to be reduced by them." Her writing gave language to experiences that had never been spoken aloud in literature. Research: I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings · Gather Together in My Name · all seven autobiographies
1931–2019 · USA
Toni Morrison
Nobel Prize-winning author · Editor · "Beloved"
Nobel Prize in Literature 1993. Her novel Beloved — about the aftermath of slavery — is one of the most important novels ever written in the English language. She was also a senior editor at Random House for almost twenty years, where she published the work of Angela Davis, Muhammad Ali, and Toni Cade Bambara. She said: "If you have some power, then your job is to empower somebody else." Research: Beloved · Song of Solomon · The Bluest Eye · Nobel Lecture 1993
1889–1960 · Jamaica & USA
Zora Neale Hurston
Anthropologist · Folklorist · Author · Harlem Renaissance figure
Trained anthropologist who documented African American and Caribbean folklore, music, and cultural traditions at a time when they were being dismissed as primitive. Her novel Their Eyes Were Watching God was largely ignored in her lifetime — she died in poverty. Alice Walker rediscovered her work in the 1970s. She is now recognised as one of the most important American writers of the twentieth century. She refused to make Black life tragic for white consumption. Research: Their Eyes Were Watching God · Mules and Men · Tell My Horse
1926–1972 · Ghana
Kwame Nkrumah
Political philosopher · First President of Ghana · Pan-Africanist
Led Ghana to independence in 1957 — the first sub-Saharan African country to gain independence from colonial rule. His vision went beyond one nation: he believed in a United States of Africa and worked to make it happen. His concept of "neo-colonialism" — political independence without economic independence — named a reality that is still being lived today. He was overthrown in a CIA-backed coup in 1966 while he was away. Research: Neo-Colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism — Kwame Nkrumah · Ghana: The Autobiography of Kwame Nkrumah
1924–1987 · Jamaica
Bob Marley
Musician · Prophet · Cultural ambassador of the African diaspora
Reggae as a form carried the philosophy of Rastafari — a Black liberation theology rooted in Marcus Garvey's Pan-Africanism, Kemetic spirituality, and resistance to Babylon (the oppressive system). Marley's music reached audiences in Africa, the Caribbean, Europe, and Asia simultaneously, spreading a message of Black dignity, sovereignty, and love. He survived an assassination attempt in 1976 and performed two nights later. He said: "The people who are trying to make this world worse aren't taking a day off. How can I?" Research: Catch a Fire: The Life of Bob Marley — Timothy White
1952–2023 · USA
bell hooks
Cultural critic · Feminist · Author of 40 books
One of the most important cultural critics of the twentieth century. Her work linked race, gender, class, and capitalism into a unified analysis of how oppression works — and how love, specifically, is the force that dismantles it. Her book All About Love is the most practical philosophical text on what it actually means to love as a political act. She wrote for everyone — her books were never academic gatekept. Research: All About Love · Ain't I a Woman · Teaching to Transgress · The Will to Change
3100 BCE – 600 CE

Ancient Africa — the first civilisations

The world's oldest civilisations were African. Before Greece, before Rome, before any European power — there was Kemet, Nubia, Carthage, Aksum. Everything that came after borrowed from what was built here.

c.3100 BCE
King Narmer unifies Upper and Lower Egypt — Kemet is born
Nile Valley, North Africa
▼
The first nation-state in recorded history. Narmer — also known as Menes — unified the two kingdoms of Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing a centralised state that would endure for over 3,000 years. He is depicted on the Narmer Palette, one of the earliest historical documents. This was not conquest for conquest's sake: unification created the conditions for one of the most sophisticated civilisations Earth has ever seen.
Why it matters: 3,000 years of continuous civilisation. Every institution we are trying to build — governance, medicine, education, economics, spirituality integrated as one — was functioning in Kemet. This is not inspiration. This is proof.
Research: The Oxford History of Ancient Egypt — Ian Shaw (ed.) · Kemet and the African Worldview — Maulana Karenga
c.2650 BCE
Imhotep designs the Step Pyramid — the world's first large-scale stone structure
Saqqara, Kemet
▼
Imhotep was chief architect, physician, and philosopher to Pharaoh Djoser. He designed the Step Pyramid — the first large-scale stone construction in human history. He was also the world's first named physician, documenting medical practice in scrolls that influenced Egyptian medicine for 2,000 years. He was deified after death and worshipped as a god of medicine for 3,000 years. The Greeks renamed him Asclepius. History renamed the credit.
Why it matters: One man — architect, doctor, philosopher, engineer simultaneously. This is what the New Earth's integrated education system produces: people who are not one thing but many, whose knowledge serves the community in multiple dimensions at once.
Research: Imhotep the African — Molefi Kete Asante · Ancient Egyptian Science — Marshall Clagett
c.2700 BCE
Merit-Ptah — the first named woman in the history of science
Kemet
▼
Chief physician to the Pharaoh — the first woman in recorded history known by name to have practised medicine. Her image in her son's tomb is inscribed "chief physician." This is 2,000 years before Hippocrates. Her existence tells you something profound about Kemet: a society governed by Ma'at — cosmic balance and truth — does not waste talent based on gender. Competence was the qualification.
Why it matters: The New Earth does not waste the gifts of half its people. The divine feminine is not separate from governance, medicine, or leadership. Merit-Ptah is the ancestral proof.
Research: Women in Science — H.J. Mozans · Ancient Egyptian Medical Papyri (Ebers, Edwin Smith, Kahun)
c.1550 BCE
The Ebers Papyrus — 842 medical remedies documented
Thebes, Kemet
▼
A 110-page scroll, 20 metres long, containing 842 medical remedies covering internal medicine, dermatology, gynaecology, dentistry, and psychiatry. Herbs documented include aloe vera, cannabis, myrrh, frankincense, garlic, castor oil, senna, henna, juniper, opium, and thyme. It documents the circulatory system with the heart at its centre — 3,500 years before William Harvey is credited with "discovering" it. Still in existence. Held at the University of Leipzig.
Why it matters: This is Dr Sebi's entire philosophy — plants as medicine, the body as a system to be understood not suppressed — documented 3,500 years ago. The knowledge was always there.
Research: Ebers Papyrus — University of Leipzig digital archive · Herbal Medicine in Ancient Egypt — Lans et al.
c.1600 BCE
Edwin Smith Papyrus — the world's oldest surgical textbook
Kemet
▼
48 cases of trauma documented with clinical precision — head injuries, spinal fractures, dislocations, tumours. First text in history to use the word "brain" and describe its function. Completely rational, evidence-based — no magic, only observation. Likely based on texts from the Old Kingdom, making it potentially 5,000 years old. Originally attributed to Imhotep. Acquired by Edwin Smith in 1862. Now held at the New York Academy of Medicine.
Why it matters: 5,000 years ago, African physicians were performing surgery with documented protocols. The "primitive" narrative is not just wrong — it is a deliberate inversion of the historical record.
Research: The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus Vol.1 — James Henry Breasted (Oriental Institute, University of Chicago)
814 BCE
Dido of Carthage founds the city — one of the ancient world's greatest trading powers
Carthage (modern Tunisia)
▼
Dido (also known as Elissa) was a Phoenician princess who fled a murderous brother and founded Carthage on the North African coast. The city became one of the Mediterranean's dominant powers, controlling trade routes across the western Mediterranean. Hannibal — one of history's greatest military commanders — was Carthaginian. Mago of Carthage wrote the world's first agricultural encyclopaedia. When Rome finally destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, it ordered Mago's work translated into Latin immediately — because even in destruction, they recognised the knowledge was too valuable to lose.
Why it matters: An African woman founded one of the ancient world's most powerful cities. The history of Africa before colonialism is a history of power, sovereignty, and genius — not absence.
Research: Carthage Must Be Destroyed — Richard Miles · Encyclopaedia Britannica on Carthage
c.100–940 CE
Kingdom of Aksum — Africa's first Christian empire and Red Sea trade power
Ethiopia & Eritrea
▼
One of the four great powers of the ancient world alongside Rome, Persia, and China — as described by the Persian prophet Mani in the 3rd century CE. Aksum controlled Red Sea trade routes between the Mediterranean, Arabia, and India. It produced its own currency — one of only five kingdoms in the ancient world to do so. It adopted Christianity in the 4th century CE, before most of Europe. Its currency was gold. Its architecture included the Aksumite stelae — granite towers up to 33 metres tall — some of which are still standing.
Why it matters: Africa was not on the periphery of the ancient world. It was at the centre of it — connected, trading, innovating, and governing sovereign states for millennia.
Research: The Kingdom of Aksum — Stuart Munro-Hay · Ancient Ethiopia — David Phillipson
600 CE – 1600 CE

Medieval Africa & the Islamic golden age

While Europe was in the Dark Ages, Africa was in a golden one. The Mali Empire, Timbuktu, the Songhai Empire, Great Zimbabwe, and the Swahili Coast were building civilisations of extraordinary sophistication.

989 CE
The University of Sankore founded in Timbuktu
Timbuktu, Mali
▼
Founded by Al-Qadi Aqib Ibn Mahmud — a judge and scholar. The university grew from a mosque into one of the world's most important centres of learning. At its peak: 25,000 students in a city of 100,000. Four degree levels, the highest taking 10 years to complete. Subjects: law, medicine, astronomy, mathematics, physics, chemistry, philosophy, geography, history, art, and practical trades. Oxford was founded 107 years later and had a fraction of the students. The intellectual freedom enjoyed in Western universities was directly inspired by institutions like Sankore.
Why it matters: The Kin-nect library, the study circles, the knowledge commons — this is what they are building toward. Timbuktu did it first and for centuries. We know it works.
Research: Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire — John Hunwick · The World-Class University of Sankore — Piga (ResearchGate)
1235 CE
Sundiata Keita founds the Mali Empire after the Battle of Kirina
West Africa
▼
Sundiata Keita — the "Lion King" — defeated Sumanguru Kante of the Sosso Empire at the Battle of Kirina and founded the Mali Empire. His story is preserved in the Sundiata Epic — one of the great oral epics of world literature, still performed by griots (oral historians) today. The Mali Empire at its peak covered an area larger than Western Europe. Its founding was not just military: Sundiata established a constitution called the Kouroukan Fouga, which guaranteed rights to women, prohibited slavery for certain groups, and enshrined environmental protection. Written down in the 1990s. Predates Magna Carta by 22 years.
Why it matters: The Kouroukan Fouga is a constitution from 1235 that included environmental protections and women's rights. Our ancestors had this figured out 800 years ago.
Research: Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali — D.T. Niane · The Oral Epic of Sundiata — performed traditions
1324 CE
Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca — the wealthiest journey in history
Mali to Mecca
▼
Mansa Musa set out for Mecca with 60,000 people including 12,000 personal servants and a baggage train carrying tonnes of gold. He gave gold away freely along the route — to the poor, to foreign dignitaries, to mosques. The sheer quantity of gold he introduced into the economies of Egypt and the Middle East caused inflation that lasted twelve years. His generosity alone disrupted the global economy. He used his wealth on return to commission the Djinguereber Mosque, expand the universities, and import scholars from across the Islamic world.
Why it matters: The wealthiest person in recorded history was African. He used his wealth to build knowledge infrastructure. This is the model: wealth in service of community, not personal accumulation.
Research: Mansa Musa and the Empire of Mali — P. James Oliver · Timbuktu and the Songhay Empire — John Hunwick
c.1100–1450 CE
Great Zimbabwe — the largest stone structure in sub-Saharan Africa
Zimbabwe
▼
A city of 18,000 people at its peak, governed by the Shona people. Its stone enclosures — built without mortar — include walls 11 metres high and 5 metres thick. At its centre, the Great Enclosure is the largest ancient structure in sub-Saharan Africa. It controlled the gold trade routes between the interior and the Swahili Coast ports. When European colonisers first encountered it in the 19th century, they refused to believe Africans had built it — attributing it to Phoenicians, Arabs, anyone but the Shona. The buildings were literally signed by their builders in the stone.
Why it matters: Natural building, community at scale, trade sovereignty — Great Zimbabwe had all of it. The country named itself after this place in 1980. The ancestors left their names in stone.
Research: Great Zimbabwe — Peter Garlake · Zimbabwe Before 1890 — D.N. Beach
1375 CE
Ibn Khaldun writes the Muqaddimah — the world's first social science
North Africa (born in Tunisia)
▼
Ibn Khaldun's Muqaddimah (Introduction to History) is the world's first systematic work of sociology, economics, and political science. He developed theories of social cohesion (asabiyyah), the rise and fall of civilisations, economic cycles, and the relationship between climate and culture — 400 years before European scholars would attempt the same. His analysis of why civilisations collapse is more sophisticated than most modern political theory. Available in English translation. Mandatory reading for anyone building a community.
Why it matters: He identified exactly the patterns — internal cohesion, economic exploitation, corruption of leadership — that destroy communities. Understanding them is how you build one that lasts.
Research: The Muqaddimah — Ibn Khaldun (trans. Franz Rosenthal, Princeton University Press)
1600 CE – 1900 CE

Diaspora & resistance

The period of the transatlantic slave trade and its aftermath. Not a history of victimhood — a history of resistance, survival, and the extraordinary things built against every possible obstacle.

1600s–present
The Maroon communities — free cities in the mountains
Jamaica, Suriname, Brazil, USA
▼
Across the Caribbean and Americas, enslaved Africans escaped and built free communities — the Maroons of Jamaica, the Quilombos of Brazil (Palmares had 30,000 people and lasted 100 years), the Saramaka and Ndyuka of Suriname (still sovereign peoples today). They maintained African governance, spiritual practices, food systems, and languages. Queen Nanny defeated the British so decisively they signed a peace treaty in 1739. The Suriname Maroons signed a similar treaty in 1762. These were sovereign states built by people who had been enslaved.
Why it matters: Every argument that Black people "can't" build self-sufficient communities is answered by the Maroons. They built them with nothing, in hostile terrain, against an empire. We have more.
Research: The Maroons of Jamaica — Barbara Kopytoff · Maroon Societies — Richard Price (ed.)
1739 CE
Treaty of Cudjoe — Britain legally recognises Maroon sovereignty
Jamaica
▼
After decades of guerrilla warfare in which the Jamaican Maroons — under Captain Cudjoe and the spiritual leadership of Queen Nanny — consistently defeated British regular forces, Britain signed a peace treaty. The treaty granted the Maroons land rights, freedom, and self-governance in perpetuity in exchange for not harbouring newly escaped enslaved people. It was a compromise. But it was also a British Empire legally acknowledging that a community of formerly enslaved Africans had defeated it. That has never been taught in British schools.
Why it matters: Diplomatic sovereignty is possible. The New Earth does not only build — it negotiates, strategically, from a position of demonstrated power.
Research: The Maroons of Jamaica — Carey Robinson · Treaty text available via National Library of Jamaica
1791–1804 CE
Haitian Revolution — the only successful slave revolt in history
Haiti (then Saint-Domingue)
▼
Enslaved people in Saint-Domingue (now Haiti) rose up in 1791, led by Toussaint L'Ouverture, Jean-Jacques Dessalines, and Henri Christophe. They defeated the armies of France, Britain, and Spain. Napoleon sent 86,000 soldiers — they were defeated. Haiti declared independence on January 1, 1804, becoming the first Black republic in the world and the second republic in the Western Hemisphere. France then forced Haiti to pay reparations for the "property" (enslaved people) that had freed itself — a debt not fully paid until 1947. Haiti has been economically strangled for its audacity ever since.
Why it matters: Haiti proved it was possible. The entire Western world spent the next two centuries making sure it paid a price for that proof — which tells you exactly how threatening it was.
Research: The Black Jacobins — C.L.R. James · Haiti: The Aftershocks of History — Laurent Dubois
1825–1857 CE
Seneca Village — a Black community where Central Park now stands
Manhattan, New York
▼
A 25-year-old shoeshiner named Andrew Williams paid $125 for three lots of land in upper Manhattan in 1825 — two years before slavery was abolished in New York State. Within 30 years: 50 homes, 3 churches, 2 schools, gardens, livestock, a natural spring, and a community that owned its land and therefore its right to vote. Demolished by the city in 1857 for Central Park. Residents called squatters. No photographs exist. The city changed the zoning laws to prevent rebuilding. Archaeologists confirmed the site in 2011.
Why it matters: The minimum viable community — land, water, food, school, church — can be built in 30 years from $125. And it will be destroyed if it is not legally protected. Both lessons are essential.
Research: Before Central Park — Sara Cedar Miller · New York Public Library Seneca Village archive
1865 CE onwards
The Freedom Colonies — hundreds of land-based communities built by freed people
American South
▼
Immediately after emancipation, formerly enslaved people pooled resources and bought land. In Texas alone, over 500 Freedom Colonies were established. They built churches first, schools second, farms third. Eatonville, Florida — founded 1887 — is the oldest continuously inhabited Black township in the United States. Most Freedom Colonies were eventually destroyed by land theft, highway construction, or economic strangulation. A few survive. Royal, Florida — under threat from turnpike extension today — is described as "one of the last remaining Black communities left largely intact since emancipation."
Why it matters: Formerly enslaved people with nothing immediately understood: land first. Church. School. Food. In that order. They were right then. They are right now.
Research: Freedom Colonies: Independent Black Texans in the Time of Jim Crow — Andrea Roberts
1900 CE – 2000 CE

The modern era

The twentieth century: economic sovereignty built and destroyed, independence won across the continent, the Harlem Renaissance, the civil rights era, Caribbean independence, and the global spread of Black cultural and intellectual power.

1906–1921 CE
Black Wall Street — the most prosperous Black community in American history
Greenwood District, Tulsa, Oklahoma
▼
O.W. Gurley bought 40 acres and sold only to Black people. Within 15 years: 300+ businesses, 2 schools, 2 newspapers, banks, hospitals, law firms, hotels, cinemas. The dollar circulated 19–36 times before leaving the community. Every professional service a community needs — inside the community. Destroyed June 1, 1921 by a white mob aided by the Oklahoma National Guard. 300+ killed. 10,000 homeless. $150 million in today's money destroyed. Zoning laws changed to prevent rebuilding. Never taught in schools until the late 1990s.
Why it matters: The circulating dollar. Every service inside. Conscious economic loyalty. This is the economic model of the New Earth — proven to work, proven to be targeted.
Research: The Burning — Tim Madigan · Death in a Promised Land — Scott Ellsworth · 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre Commission Report
1920s CE
The Harlem Renaissance — Black cultural explosion
New York City, USA
▼
A flowering of Black artistic, literary, and intellectual life centred on Harlem, New York. Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Claude McKay, Countee Cullen, Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, Paul Robeson — an unprecedented concentration of Black genius creating a new cultural identity. The Harlem Renaissance was also a political statement: that Black life had beauty, depth, sophistication, and joy that the dominant culture was not able to see or did not want to acknowledge. It planted seeds that grew into the civil rights movement, the Black Arts Movement, and every major Black cultural expression since.
Why it matters: Culture is not decoration. The Harlem Renaissance proves that when Black people are given space and resources to create on their own terms, the result changes the world.
Research: The Harlem Renaissance — Nathan Huggins · When Harlem Was in Vogue — David Levering Lewis
1957 CE
Ghana gains independence — Africa's decolonisation begins
Ghana, West Africa
▼
Kwame Nkrumah led Ghana to independence from Britain on March 6, 1957 — the first sub-Saharan African country to gain independence. His declaration: "We are going to demonstrate to the world, to the other nations, young as we are, that we are prepared to lay our own foundation." Between 1957 and 1966, 32 more African countries gained independence. Nkrumah's vision was a United States of Africa — political independence without economic independence (what he called neo-colonialism) was not enough. He was overthrown in a CIA-backed coup in 1966. His analysis proved correct.
Why it matters: Political independence without economic independence is not freedom. The New Earth must be economically self-sufficient — not just politically autonomous. Nkrumah named this lesson in 1965.
Research: Neo-Colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism — Kwame Nkrumah · Ghana: The Autobiography of Kwame Nkrumah
1948–1971 CE
The Windrush Generation arrives in Britain
Caribbean to UK
▼
Between 1948 and 1971, approximately 500,000 people from the Caribbean arrived in Britain — invited to help rebuild after World War II. They came to a country that had depended on their colonies for wealth and their people for labour, and found a country that didn't want them. They built communities anyway — in Notting Hill, Brixton, Handsworth, Moss Side, St Pauls. They created sound system culture, the carnival, Black British literature, the foundation of UK reggae, jungle, drum and bass, grime. The culture the world associates with Britain — particularly its music — was made by the children and grandchildren of Windrush.
Why it matters: In hostile territory, with limited resources, the community built culture. Culture sustained identity. Identity sustained community. The New Earth is being built by the grandchildren of these people.
Research: Small Island — Andrea Levy · Windrush: The Irresistible Rise of Multi-Racial Britain — Trevor Phillips & Mike Phillips
1994 CE
South Africa's first democratic election — Mandela becomes President
South Africa
▼
Nelson Mandela, after 27 years in prison on Robben Island, became the first Black President of South Africa on May 10, 1994. The transition from apartheid to democracy was one of the most watched political events in history. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission — chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu — used restorative justice principles rooted in Ubuntu philosophy to process the trauma of apartheid without civil war. It was imperfect. But it was a model of how a society can look honestly at its history and try to heal rather than only punish.
Why it matters: The TRC is the Ubuntu model of restorative justice applied at national scale. The New Earth's conflict resolution practice is rooted in exactly this: truth, restoration, and community healing.
Research: Long Walk to Freedom — Nelson Mandela · No Future Without Forgiveness — Desmond Tutu
2000 CE – present

Now — and what comes next

The work continues. Across the diaspora, across the continent, people are building — platforms, communities, businesses, movements. This is the moment the timeline has been building toward.

2003 CE
Maggie Walker's St Luke Bank legacy — the cooperative banking model
Richmond, Virginia (legacy)
▼
Maggie Lena Walker founded the St Luke Penny Savings Bank in 1903 with the explicit philosophy: "Let us put our money together; let us use our money; let us put our money out at usury among ourselves." Her bank grew to $645,000 in assets. It merged with other banks and eventually became Consolidated Bank and Trust — the oldest continuously operating African American bank in the United States, still operating today. The cooperative banking model she established is the blueprint for the New Earth's financial infrastructure: community-owned, community-serving, wealth circulating within.
Why it matters: Community banking that has survived for over 100 years. Cooperative finance is not utopian — it has a 120-year track record.
Research: Maggie Lena Walker — Gertrude Marlowe · National Park Service — Maggie L. Walker National Historic Site
2012–2022 CE
The Timbuktu manuscripts saved — 350,000 documents evacuated from jihadist forces
Mali, West Africa
▼
When jihadist forces occupied Timbuktu in 2012, Dr Abdel Kader Haidara organised the secret evacuation of 350,000 manuscripts to Bamako in the south — hiding them in trunks, transporting them on donkeys and in cars, house by house. 4,203 manuscripts were burned before the evacuation was complete. By 2022, 40,000 pages had been digitised and made available via Google Arts & Culture's "Mali Magic" portal. The knowledge that families had protected for centuries — some buried in the desert — now exists online. You can read it.
Why it matters: The ancestors hid manuscripts to save them. Dr Haidara moved them by donkey in the night. Now they are on your phone. Access: artsandculture.google.com/project/mali-magic
Research: The Bad-Ass Librarians of Timbuktu — Joshua Hammer · Google Arts & Culture: Mali Magic portal
